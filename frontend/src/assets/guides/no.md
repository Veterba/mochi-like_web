# Alphabet, Pronunciation, Basic Words <!-- slug: alphabet-pronunciation-basics -->

## The Alphabet
Norwegian uses 29 letters: the English 26 **plus æ, ø, å** (at the end of the alphabet, in that order). This guide covers **Bokmål**, the written standard used by ~85–90% of Norwegians.

## Key pronunciation points
- **æ** ≈ the *a* in English "cat" (*lære* – to learn). **ø** ≈ the vowel in "bird" with rounded lips (*øl* – beer). **å** ≈ *o* in "or" (*år* – year).
- **o** often sounds like English "oo" (*bok* ≈ "book"), while **u** is a tighter, more fronted "oo" (*hus* – house). **y** is like "ee" said with rounded lips (*by* – city) — not the same as *i*.
- **kj** /ç/ — soft sound like a whispered "h" in "huge": *kjøpe* (buy), *kjære* (dear). Many young natives merge it with skj — but learn the distinction.
- **skj / sj / sk before i,y** = English "sh": *skjorte* (shirt), *sjø* (sea), *ski* (yes, "shee").
- **r** is tapped/rolled in most of the country. **rs** often merges into "sh" (*norsk* ≈ "noshk").
- **Silent letters**: final **-t** in the definite neuter (*huset* → "huse"), **h** before j/v (*hva, hjem*), **d** after long vowels and in -ld/-nd (*god, land*), **g** in -ig (*hyggelig* → "hyggeli").
- **Long vs short vowels** are meaning-changing and tied to spelling: vowel + one consonant = long vowel (*tak* – roof), vowel + double consonant = short (*takk* – thanks).
- **Pitch accent (tones)**: word pairs like *bønder* (farmers) vs *bønner* (beans) differ by melody. Don't obsess — context nearly always saves you; just imitate the "singing" of natives.

## Basic words
- Greetings: hei, hallo, god morgen, god dag, god kveld, ha det (bra) — bye, vi ses — see you.
- Politeness: takk (thanks), tusen takk (thanks a lot), vær så snill (please — used far less than English "please"!), vær så god (here you are / you're welcome), unnskyld (sorry/excuse me), ja, nei.
- Numbers 1–10: en, to, tre, fire, fem, seks, sju, åtte, ni, ti.
- Days: mandag, tirsdag, onsdag, torsdag, fredag, lørdag, søndag (not capitalized!).
- Survival: Hva heter du? (What's your name?) — Jeg heter… / Hvordan går det? (How are you?) — Det går bra. / Jeg forstår ikke. / Kan du gjenta? / Snakker du engelsk? / Hva betyr … på norsk?

# Nouns, Gender and Articles <!-- slug: articles-and-nouns -->

## Three genders
Every noun has a gender: **masculine (en), feminine (ei), neuter (et)**: *en bil* (a car), *ei jente* (a girl), *et hus* (a house).
- In Bokmål, every feminine noun **may** be treated as masculine (*en jente / jenta* or *en jente / jenten*) — many Oslo speakers do this. Safe strategy: learn common feminines (jente, bok, dør, uke, hytte) with *ei/-a*, treat the rest as en-words.
- Gender is mostly unpredictable — **always learn nouns with their article**: not "hus" but "et hus".

## The article system — the big difference from English <!-- slug: articles -->
The **indefinite** article is a separate word before the noun, but the **definite** article is a **suffix glued onto the noun**:

| | masculine | feminine | neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| a/an | en bil | ei bok | et hus |
| the | bil**en** | bok**a** | hus**et** |
| plural | biler | bøker | hus |
| the (plural) | bil**ene** | bøk**ene** | hus**ene/husa** |

- *bilen* = the car, *huset* = the house (final -t silent!).
- **Plurals**: most nouns take **-er** (bil → biler); short neuter nouns often take nothing (et hus → flere hus); irregulars to memorize: *mann → menn, barn → barn, bok → bøker, hånd → hender, tre → trær, øye → øyne*.
- **Double definiteness**: with an adjective, Norwegian marks "the" **twice** — a front word *den/det/de* AND the suffix: *den røde bilen* (the red car), *det store huset*, *de gamle bøkene*.
- **Zero article** where English uses one: professions and nationalities after være/bli: *Hun er lærer* (She is **a** teacher), *Han er nordmann*.
- General statements use the definite form more than English: *Jeg liker kaffen her* or bare form *Jeg liker kaffe*.

# Pronouns <!-- slug: pronouns -->

| Subject | Object | Possessive |
|---|---|---|
| jeg (I) | meg | min/mi/mitt/mine |
| du (you) | deg | din/di/ditt/dine |
| han (he) | ham/han | hans |
| hun (she) | henne | hennes |
| den/det (it) | den/det | dens/dets |
| vi (we) | oss | vår/vårt/våre |
| dere (you pl.) | dere | deres |
| de (they) | dem | deres |

- **den vs det for "it"**: *den* replaces en/ei-words, *det* replaces et-words: *Bilen? Den er ny. Huset? Det er gammelt.*
- **det** is also the dummy subject everywhere: *Det regner* (It's raining), *Det er et problem* (There is a problem).
- **Possessives agree** with the noun's gender/number: *min bil, mitt hus, mine bøker*. Position: before the noun with bare form (*min bil*) or — very common and neutral — **after the noun in definite form**: *bilen min, huset mitt, boka mi*.
- **The sin/si/sitt/sine rule** (no English equivalent — this one earns real fluency points): use *sin* when the owner is the **subject of the same clause**: *Han vasker bilen **sin*** = He washes **his own** car; *Han vasker bilen **hans*** = He washes **someone else's** (another man's) car.
- Demonstratives: *denne/dette/disse* (this/these) + definite noun: *denne boka, dette huset*; *den/det/de* (that/those): *den mannen*.
- Indefinites: *noen* (some/any/somebody), *noe* (something), *ingen/ingenting* (nobody/nothing), *alle* (everybody), *alt* (everything), *man/en* (generic "one/you": *Man kan ikke røyke her*).

# Question Formation <!-- slug: question-formation -->

Beautifully simple compared to English — **no do-support exists**. Just invert verb and subject:
- *Du snakker norsk. → **Snakker du** norsk?* (Do you speak Norwegian?)
- *Hun kommer i dag. → Kommer hun i dag?*

**Question words**: hva (what), hvem (who), hvor (where), når (when), hvorfor (why), hvordan (how), hvilken/hvilket/hvilke (which), hvor mye/mange (how much/many).
- Question word first, verb second: *Hva heter du? Hvor bor du? Hvorfor lærer du norsk?*
- Answering negative questions has a special "yes": **jo**. *Snakker du ikke norsk? — **Jo!*** (= yes I do). *ja* answers positive questions, *jo* contradicts negative ones.

# Present Tense <!-- slug: present-tenses -->

One form does the work of all four English present tenses. **Verb stem + -r**, same for every person:
*jeg/du/han/vi/dere/de **snakker*** — I speak / she speaks / we speak. No third-person -s problem, no conjugation tables.

- *Jeg bor i Oslo* = I live in Oslo. *Jeg leser* = I read **or** I am reading — context decides.
- **Expressing English continuous** when you really need "in progress": *holder på å* + infinitive (*Jeg holder på å lage middag* – I'm (busy) making dinner) or *driver og* + verb (*Han driver og pusser opp* – he's renovating). Also position verbs: *Hun **sitter og** leser* (she's sitting reading), *står og venter, ligger og sover*.
- Present covers the near future with a time word: *Jeg kommer i morgen* (I'm coming tomorrow).
- Irregular presents worth memorizing: *er* (am/is/are), *har* (have), *gjør* (do), *vet* (know), *sier* (say), *spør* (ask).

# Past Tenses <!-- slug: past-tenses -->

## Preteritum (simple past) <!-- slug: past-simple -->
Verb classes by ending — learn each verb's class with the verb:
- -et: *snakke → snakket* (spoke)
- -te: *lese → leste* (read), *kjøpe → kjøpte*
- -de: *leve → levde*, *prøve → prøvde*
- -dde: *bo → bodde* (lived), *nå → nådde*
- Strong/irregular (memorize): *er–var, har–hadde, går–gikk, får–fikk, tar–tok, ser–så, kommer–kom, drikker–drakk, finner–fant, gir–ga*.

**Use**: completed actions at a stated/finished time: *Jeg **kjøpte** bilen i fjor* (I bought the car last year). Also — unlike English — for spontaneous reactions: *Det var godt!* (This is/was tasty!).

## Perfektum (present perfect) <!-- slug: present-perfect -->
**har + past participle**: *Jeg har spist* (I have eaten), *Har du vært i Norge?*
- Participles: snakket, lest, bodd, vært, gjort, sett, kommet, tatt.
- **Use is close to English**: experience (*Jeg har lest den boka*), result now (*Jeg har mistet nøklene*), unfinished period (*Jeg har bodd her i fem år*). Rule of thumb: specific finished time → preteritum; relevance now → perfektum. Norwegian is slightly more relaxed than English about this line, but the English instinct works.
- Movement/change verbs may take *er* in careful style: *Han er gått* — but modern Bokmål mostly uses *har* everywhere: *Han har gått.*

## Pluskvamperfektum (past perfect) <!-- slug: past-perfect -->
**hadde + participle** — past before the past, exactly like English: *Da vi kom, hadde filmen begynt* (When we arrived, the film had started).

*(No continuous forms exist in any past tense — "I was reading when…" = *Jeg **satt og leste** da…* or plain *Jeg leste da…*)*

# Future <!-- slug: future-tenses -->

Three tools, and the choice carries meaning:
- **skal + infinitive** — plans, intentions, agreements (someone decided): *Jeg skal reise til Bergen i morgen.* (I'm going to / I will travel…)
- **vil + infinitive** — predictions, and "want to": *Det vil bli kaldt i vinter* (it will get cold). ⚠️ *Jeg vil ha kaffe* = I **want** coffee, not "I will have". English speakers overuse *vil* for future — when in doubt about plans, use *skal*.
- **kommer til å + infinitive** — neutral prediction, what's going to happen: *Det kommer til å regne* (It's going to rain). Closest to "going to".
- **Present tense + time word** is the most common everyday future: *Vi drar klokka åtte* (We leave at eight). When English says "will", Norwegian very often just uses present.

# Word Order — the V2 Rule <!-- slug: word-order-v2 -->
*(Language-specific chapter: this is the #1 grammar topic in Norwegian.)*

**Main clauses: the finite verb is ALWAYS the second element.** If anything other than the subject starts the sentence, subject and verb swap:
- *Jeg spiser frokost klokka åtte.* → *Klokka åtte **spiser jeg** frokost.* → *Nå **kommer hun**.*
- ✗ ~~I morgen jeg skal jobbe~~ → ✓ *I morgen skal jeg jobbe.* This is THE most common learner mistake — drill it until automatic.

**Negation**: *ikke* comes after the finite verb (and after object pronouns): *Jeg snakker **ikke** fransk. Jeg liker det ikke.*

**Subordinate clauses** (after at, fordi, hvis, som, når…): no inversion, and **ikke moves BEFORE the verb**:
- Main: *Hun kommer **ikke**.* → Sub: *Jeg vet at hun **ikke** kommer.*
- Memory aid: in subordinate clauses the order is subject → ikke/adverb → verb.

**If a subordinate clause starts the sentence, it counts as element #1**, so main clause inverts: *Hvis det regner, **blir vi** hjemme.*

# Modal Verbs <!-- slug: modal-verbs -->

## Structure <!-- slug: modals-structure -->
Modal + **infinitive without å**: *Jeg kan svømme* ✗ ~~kan å svømme~~. Negative: modal + ikke: *Du må ikke gå.* Questions by inversion: *Kan du hjelpe meg?*

Presents/pasts: kan–kunne, vil–ville, skal–skulle, må–måtte, bør–burde.

## The core modals <!-- slug: can-could -->
- **kan** = can (ability, permission, possibility): *Kan jeg få en kaffe?* (Can I have a coffee — the standard ordering phrase). *kunne* = could: *Kunne du gjenta det?* (polite).
- **må** = must / have to: *Jeg må jobbe i dag.* ⚠️ **må ikke** usually means "must not" in careful usage but colloquially often "don't have to" — to be unambiguous, say *trenger ikke (å)* for "don't have to": *Du trenger ikke å betale.* <!-- slug: must-have-to -->
- **skal** = shall/is to (plans, obligations set by someone): *Du skal være hjemme klokka ti.*
- **vil** = want to (and prediction): *Vil du bli med?* (Do you want to come along?)
- **bør / burde** = should: *Du bør slutte å røyke. Du burde ha sagt det* (you should have said it — burde ha + participle). <!-- slug: should -->
- **får** = get to / may: *Du får ikke lov* (you're not allowed); *får* + infinitive ≈ "I'll…": *Jeg får se* (I'll see).

# Adjectives and Adverbs <!-- slug: adjectives-adverbs -->

**Adjectives agree** — the price for the easy verbs:
- en-word: *en **stor** bil*; et-word: **+t**: *et **stort** hus*; plural: **+e**: *store biler*.
- **Definite form always -e**: *den store bilen, det store huset, de store bilene* — and after possessives: *min nye bil / den nye bilen min*.
- Predicative also agrees: *Bilen er stor. Huset er stort. Bilene er store.*
- Exceptions: adjectives in -ig take no -t (*et hyggelig hus*); *bra, moderne* never change; *liten* is wildly irregular: *liten/lita/lite/små + lille* (definite): *en liten bil, et lite hus, små barn, den lille jenta*.

**Adverbs**: often = neuter adjective (add -t): *Han kjører **pent*** (he drives nicely). *God → godt, dårlig → dårlig.*
- Location vs movement pairs — a Norwegian specialty: *hjemme* (at home) vs *hjem* (homewards), *inne/inn, ute/ut, oppe/opp, nede/ned, her/hit, der/dit*: *Jeg er hjemme* but *Jeg går hjem.*

**Comparison**: -ere / -est: *stor – større – størst, pen – penere – penest*; long adjectives with *mer/mest*: *mer interessant*. Irregulars: *god – bedre – best, dårlig – verre – verst, gammel – eldre – eldst, mye – mer – mest, mange – flere – flest, liten – mindre – minst*.
- *enn* = than: *Hun er eldre enn meg. like … som* = as … as: *like stor som*.

# Prepositions <!-- slug: prepositions -->

## Time <!-- slug: prepositions-time -->
- **i** + years, months, seasons, "for X time": *i 2026, i juli, i sommer, i tre år* (for three years).
- **på** + days and parts of day (habitual): *på mandag, på morgenen*; also "in X time (duration to complete)": *på ti minutter*.
- **om** + "in X from now" and habitual seasons/parts of day: *om fem minutter* (in five minutes), *om vinteren* (in winter, generally), *om natta*.
- **klokka** for clock time: *klokka åtte* (at eight — no preposition!).
- **for … siden** = ago: *for to år siden* (two years ago).
- **siden** = since: *siden 2021*.

## Place: i vs på <!-- slug: prepositions-place -->
The eternal Norwegian question:
- **i** = inside; countries, most cities: *i Norge, i Oslo, i huset, i bilen*.
- **på** = on surfaces; but ALSO institutions/activities/many places: *på bordet, på skolen, på jobb, på kino, på butikken, på fjellet, på hytta, på et hotell*; and some Norwegian towns/islands (*på Hamar, på Svalbard*).
- Rule of thumb: rooms/buildings as containers → i; functions/institutions/surfaces → på. Then memorize per phrase — natives learned them one by one too.
- Others: *ved* (by/at: ved vinduet), *hos* (at someone's place: hos legen, hos meg — like French *chez*), *mellom* (between), *ved siden av* (next to), *foran/bak* (in front of/behind), *under/over*.

## Movement <!-- slug: prepositions-movement -->
- **til** = to: *Jeg reiser til Bergen, til Norge, til jobben.* Special: *hjem* (home, no prep).
- **fra** = from: *Jeg kommer fra Ukraina. fra … til …*
- **inn i / ut av** = into / out of: *Han gikk inn i rommet.* **opp på** = onto.
- **gjennom** (through), *over* (across/over), *langs* (along), *mot* (towards).

# Conjunctions and Complex Sentences <!-- slug: conjunctions-complex-sentences -->

## Conjunctions <!-- slug: conjunctions -->
- Coordinating (no word-order effect): **og** (and), **men** (but), **eller** (or), **for** (for/because, formal), **så** (so).
- Subordinating (trigger subordinate word order — ikke before verb!): **at** (that), **fordi** (because), **hvis/dersom** (if), **når** (when, present/habitual), **da** (when, single past event), **mens** (while), **før** (before), **etter at** (after), **selv om** (even though), **som** (relative who/which/that).
- **da vs når** for "when": *Da jeg var barn…* (one past period) vs *Når jeg er trøtt…* (whenever/present/future). Mnemonic natives learn in school: "den gang **da**, hver gang **når**".

## Complex sentences <!-- slug: complex-sentences-word-order -->
- Relative **som**: *Mannen som bor der, er lærer.* Som can drop when it's the object: *Boka (som) jeg leser…*
- Indirect questions keep subordinate order: *Hvor bor han? → Jeg vet ikke hvor han **bor*** (no inversion) — same rule as English, easier to remember here.
- *om* = if/whether in indirect yes/no questions: *Jeg vet ikke om hun kommer.*

# Passive Voice <!-- slug: passive-voice -->

Two passives:
- **-s passive** (add -s to the infinitive/present): *Bilen vaskes hver uke* (the car is washed every week). Common for rules, habits, signs: *Billetter selges her. Må ikke berøres.*
- **bli-passive** (bli + participle): *Bilen ble vasket i går* (the car was washed yesterday). The default for single events, all tenses: *har blitt vasket, vil bli vasket.*
- Agent with **av**: *Boka ble skrevet av Hamsun.*
- Rule of thumb: general/repeated → -s; specific event → bli.

# Conditionals <!-- slug: conditionals -->

**hvis** = if. Norwegian mirrors English logic tense-for-tense, minus complications:
- Real: *Hvis det regner, blir vi hjemme* (if it rains, we stay/will stay home). Future in the result: *…skal/vil vi bli hjemme.* <!-- slug: first-conditional -->
- Unreal present: **hvis + preteritum, ville + infinitive**: *Hvis jeg hadde mye penger, ville jeg reise verden rundt.* <!-- slug: second-conditional -->
- Unreal past: **hvis + hadde + participle, ville ha + participle**: *Hvis jeg hadde visst det, ville jeg ha hjulpet deg.* <!-- slug: third-conditional -->
- Stylish inversion can replace hvis: *Hadde jeg visst det, ville jeg ha hjulpet.*
- Remember: the hvis-clause counts as element #1 → main clause inverts (V2).

# Particle Verbs (Phrasal Verbs) <!-- slug: phrasal-verbs -->

Norwegian loves particle verbs just like English — often with the *same* particles, a gift for English speakers:
- *gi opp* (give up), *finne ut* (find out), *slå av/på* (turn off/on), *ta av* (take off), *komme tilbake* (come back), *se fram til* (look forward to), *gå ut* (go out), *våkne opp* (wake up), *henge med* (hang out / keep up).
- **Stress the particle**, not the verb: *slå PÅ lyset.*
- Object placement: nouns after the particle (*slå på lyset*), pronouns between (*slå det på*) — same instinct as English "turn it on".
- ⚠️ Particle position can change meaning: *Han kastet ut hunden* (threw the dog out) vs *kastet opp* (vomited!). Learn as whole units.

# "used to" and "had to" <!-- slug: used-to-had-to -->

- **used to** = **pleide å + infinitive**: *Jeg pleide å røyke* (I used to smoke). *Vi pleide å bade i sjøen hver sommer.* Present habit: *Jeg pleier å stå opp klokka sju* (I usually get up at seven) — note Norwegian uses pleie in present too, unlike English!
- Or simply **før** (before/formerly) + preteritum: *Jeg røykte før.* *Det var en kino her før* (there used to be a cinema here).
- **had to** = **måtte** (past of må): *Jeg måtte jobbe i helga* (I had to work at the weekend). *Vi måtte vente i to timer.*
- "will have to" = *må* or *kommer til å måtte*; "didn't have to" = *trengte ikke (å)*: *Du trengte ikke å komme.*

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

Plot in **present tense**, like English: *Filmen **handler om** en mann som…* (the film is about a man who…).
- Facts: *Det er en komedie/thriller/dokumentar. Den er regissert av… Hovedrollen spilles av… Den er basert på en bok. Handlingen foregår i Oslo på 80-tallet.*
- Plot: *Filmen handler om… Hovedpersonen prøver å… men…*
- Opinion: *Jeg likte den veldig godt. Skuespillerne er kjempegode. Slutten var overraskende/forutsigbar. Den er verdt å se. Jeg anbefaler den.*
- Vocabulary: *en film, en skuespiller, en regissør, hovedperson, skurk (villain), handling (plot), slutten (the ending), spennende (exciting), morsom (funny), kjedelig (boring), rørende (moving), skummel (scary).*
