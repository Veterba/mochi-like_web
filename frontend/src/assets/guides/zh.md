# Pronunciation & Pinyin — the Four Tones <!-- slug: pronunciation-pinyin-tones -->

Mandarin has no alphabet. **Pinyin** is the romanization system used to write sounds — it is a learning tool, not the language. Every syllable in Mandarin has a **tone**, and the tone is as much a part of the word as the consonants and vowels. Getting tones wrong doesn't produce an accent — it produces a different word.

## The Five Tones <!-- slug: tones -->

| Tone | Mark | Description | Example |
|------|------|-------------|---------|
| 1st — high level | ā | held high and flat, like singing a sustained note | 妈 (mā) — mother |
| 2nd — rising | á | rises sharply, like asking "huh?" | 麻 (má) — hemp/numb |
| 3rd — dipping | ǎ | starts mid, dips low, then rises | 马 (mǎ) — horse |
| 4th — falling | à | drops hard and fast from high to low | 骂 (mà) — scold |
| Neutral — unstressed | a | short, light, no tone mark | 吗 (ma) — question particle |

**mā má mǎ mà ma** — five distinct words from one syllable. This is the make-or-break reality of Mandarin. The 3rd tone is hardest: it rarely gets the full dip-and-rise except in isolation; before another syllable it often just drops low.

## Tone Sandhi — when tones change in context <!-- slug: tone-sandhi -->

Two rules you must internalize:

**不 (bù, "not")** is 4th tone by default. But before another 4th-tone syllable it becomes 2nd: *bù + 去 (qù)* → **bú qù** (not going). Before any other tone it stays 4th.

**一 (yī, "one/a")** is 1st tone in isolation. Before 4th tone → 2nd: *yī + 个 (gè)* → **yí gè**. Before 1st/2nd/3rd tone → 4th: *yī + 天 (tiān)* → **yì tiān** (one day). In ordinal position or alone, stays 1st.

**Two consecutive 3rd tones**: the first becomes 2nd tone: *你好 (nǐ hǎo)* — spoken as **ní hǎo** (the first syllable rises). This is why "hello" doesn't sound like two dipping tones.

## Initials & Finals <!-- slug: initials-finals -->

Pinyin syllables = **initial** (consonant onset) + **final** (vowel nucleus + ending). Tricky ones for English speakers:

**Initials:**
- **x** = like English "sh" but with your tongue flat against the bottom teeth, not curled: *xīn* (heart)
- **q** = "ch" with tongue flat: *qǐng* (please)
- **zh/ch/sh/r** = retroflex — tongue curls back slightly: *zhōngguó* (China). Contrast with flat **z/c/s**: *zì* (character) vs *zhī* (to know)
- **j** = like English "j" but never voiced heavily: *jiā* (home)
- **r** is not rolled — it's more like an English "r" merged with "zh": *rén* (person)

**Finals:**
- **e** alone = the "uh" sound, not "eh": *hē* (drink)
- **ui** = actually "uei": *duì* (correct)
- **-n vs -ng**: *mān* (slow) vs *máng* (busy) — the nasal ending matters
- **ü** = German ü, French u — round your lips for "ee": *lǘ* (donkey). After **j/q/x/y** the dots are dropped: *jū* = *jü*

## The English-speaker trap <!-- slug: tones-trap -->

English uses pitch for **emotion and sentence grammar** (rising for questions, etc.). In Mandarin, pitch belongs to the **word**. Your brain will fight you and try to put 3rd/4th tones on words that feel like questions or uncertain statements. Consciously override this — the tone stays on the word no matter how you feel about the sentence.

✗ Common error: saying *nǐ hǎo* with a rising intonation on *hǎo* to sound "polite" — this turns it into 2nd+2nd or something undefined.
✓ Keep the tones steady; use filler words and particles for emotional tone.

# Chinese Characters — Hanzi <!-- slug: hanzi-characters -->

Characters are the written form of the language. Each character = one syllable = (usually) one morpheme. You don't need thousands to start, but you need some foundation.

## How characters are built <!-- slug: radicals-structure -->

Most characters are **compound**: they combine a **semantic radical** (hint at meaning) with a **phonetic component** (hint at pronunciation). Example:

- 语 (*yǔ* — language): radical 讠(speech/language) + 吾 (*wú*, phonetically similar) → something related to speech
- 请 (*qǐng* — please/invite): 讠(speech) + 青 (*qīng*, phonetically hints at "qing") → polite speech

**Radicals** are the left/top component that carries semantic flavor. Common ones:
- 讠= speech/language: 说, 话, 语, 请
- 氵= water: 海, 河, 洗, 泳
- 木 = wood/tree: 树, 桌, 椅, 林
- 人/亻= person: 他, 们, 做, 你
- 口 = mouth/speech: 吃, 喝, 叫, 问

Learning radicals = shortcut for both memory and guessing. If you see 氵on a character you've never met, it probably relates to water or liquid.

## Strokes <!-- slug: strokes -->

Characters are built from **strokes**, written in a fixed order (generally top→bottom, left→right). Stroke order affects legibility and handwriting fluency. The main stroke types: horizontal (一), vertical (丨), left-falling (丿), right-falling (㇏), dot (丶), hook (乚).

## Simplified vs Traditional <!-- slug: simplified-traditional -->

Mainland China uses **simplified characters** (简体字, jiǎntǐzì) — reformed in the 1950s–60s to reduce stroke counts. Taiwan, Hong Kong, and overseas communities often use **traditional characters** (繁體字, fántǐzì). This guide uses simplified.

Examples: 爱 (simplified) vs 愛 (traditional) — "love"; 书 vs 書 — "book".

## Characters map one-to-one with syllables <!-- slug: syllable-map -->

A crucial structural fact: every character is exactly one syllable. There are no multi-syllable characters. Most modern Chinese *words* are **two characters/syllables**:
- 电话 (*diànhuà*) — telephone (electric + speech)
- 朋友 (*péngyǒu*) — friend (companion + friend)
- 学习 (*xuéxí*) — study (learn + practice)

This means reading speed ≈ recognizing character-pairs, not individual symbols.

# Basic Word Order <!-- slug: word-order -->

Mandarin is fundamentally **SVO** (Subject–Verb–Object) like English, but with one deeper organizing principle: **topic-comment structure**.

## SVO and topic-comment <!-- slug: topic-comment -->

In a topic-comment sentence, whatever is being *talked about* (the topic) comes first — even if grammatically it's the object:

*这本书我看过了。(Zhè běn shū wǒ kàn guò le.)* — "This book, I've (already) read it."
— the book is the topic, the rest is the comment. Chinese does this constantly and naturally.

## Time and place before the verb <!-- slug: time-place-order -->

**Temporal and locative expressions come BEFORE the verb** — the opposite of English default:

| Chinese | Pinyin | English |
|---------|--------|---------|
| 我**明天**去 | wǒ **míngtiān** qù | I'm going tomorrow |
| 他**在北京**工作 | tā **zài Běijīng** gōngzuò | He works in Beijing |
| 我**每天早上**喝咖啡 | wǒ **měitiān zǎoshang** hē kāfēi | I drink coffee every morning |

Order within the pre-verb zone: **Subject → Time → Place → Verb → Object**. "Yesterday in Shanghai I ate dumplings" → *我昨天在上海吃了饺子。*

✗ Don't calque English: ~~*我吃了饺子在上海昨天*~~ — Chinese doesn't shuffle post-verb the way English does.

# Measure Words / Classifiers <!-- slug: measure-words -->

*(Language-specific chapter: mandatory between any number or demonstrative and a noun.)*

In English you say "three books." In Chinese you cannot say *三书* — you must insert a **measure word** (量词, liàngcí, also called classifier): **三本书** (*sān běn shū*, three [volume] books).

The same applies with 这 (zhè — this) and 那 (nà — that): 这**本**书 (this book), 那**个**人 (that person).

## 个 (gè) — the default <!-- slug: measure-word-ge -->

**个** is the most common classifier and works as a safe fallback for people and many general nouns when you can't remember the specific one. It's slightly informal but widely understood.

| Measure Word | Used for | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 个 (gè) | people, general objects (fallback) | 一个人 (one person), 两个苹果 (two apples) |
| 本 (běn) | bound books, notebooks | 三本书 (three books) |
| 张 (zhāng) | flat things: paper, tables, tickets, faces | 一张票 (one ticket), 两张桌子 (two tables) |
| 条 (tiáo) | long, flexible things: rivers, roads, fish, pants | 一条鱼 (one fish), 两条腿 (two legs) |
| 只 (zhī) | small animals, one of a pair | 一只猫 (one cat), 一只手 (one hand) |
| 件 (jiàn) | items: clothes, affairs, luggage | 一件衬衫 (one shirt) |
| 杯 (bēi) | cups/glasses of liquid | 一杯水 (one glass of water) |
| 瓶 (píng) | bottles | 一瓶啤酒 (one bottle of beer) |
| 位 (wèi) | people (polite/respectful) | 一位老师 (one teacher — respectful) |
| 把 (bǎ) | things with handles: chairs, knives, umbrellas | 一把椅子 (one chair) |

**Mnemonic**: think of measure words as telling you the *shape or category* of the object. 张 = flat surface. 条 = long and bendy. 只 = small and singular.

✗ *三书* — missing classifier
✓ *三本书* (three books)

Numbers 两 (liǎng) vs 二 (èr): use **两** before measure words (*两个人* — two people), **二** for counting in sequences (二, 三, 四...) and math.

# Pronouns & Possession with 的 <!-- slug: pronouns-possession -->

## Personal Pronouns <!-- slug: personal-pronouns -->

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | 我 wǒ (I/me) | 我们 wǒmen (we/us) |
| 2nd | 你 nǐ (you) / 您 nín (polite you) | 你们 nǐmen (you all) |
| 3rd | 他 tā (he/him) / 她 tā (she/her) / 它 tā (it) | 他们 tāmen / 她们 tāmen |

**他/她/它** sound identical in speech — all *tā*. Context resolves gender; spoken Mandarin is effectively genderless.

**您 (nín)** is the formal/respectful "you" — use with elders, strangers in service contexts, teachers.

## 的 (de) — the possession particle <!-- slug: de-possession -->

**的** after a pronoun (or noun) marks possession, like an apostrophe-s:

- 我的书 (*wǒ de shū*) — my book
- 她的朋友 (*tā de péngyǒu*) — her friend
- 中国的文化 (*Zhōngguó de wénhuà*) — China's culture / Chinese culture

Between **close relationships and short phrases**, 的 is often dropped colloquially:
- 我妈 (my mom) — not 我的妈妈 in casual speech
- 我们学校 (our school)

**的 also links adjectives to nouns** — see the Adjectives chapter.

# Verbs Never Conjugate <!-- slug: verbs-no-conjugation -->

This is genuinely liberating: **Mandarin verbs do not change form — ever.**

- No person agreement: *我去 / 你去 / 他去 / 我们去* — "I go / you go / he goes / we go" — same 去 every time
- No tense conjugation: *我去* can mean "I go," "I went," or "I will go" depending on context
- No infinitive vs finite distinction

The same verb form appears in every slot. This means **tense and aspect are handled by other means** — time words and aspect particles (see next chapter).

**Common verbs (no forms to memorize):**

| Verb | Pinyin | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 是 | shì | to be (for identity/classification) |
| 有 | yǒu | to have / there is |
| 去 | qù | to go |
| 来 | lái | to come |
| 吃 | chī | to eat |
| 喝 | hē | to drink |
| 说 | shuō | to speak/say |
| 做 | zuò | to do/make |
| 看 | kàn | to watch/read/look |
| 听 | tīng | to listen |
| 买 | mǎi | to buy |
| 知道 | zhīdào | to know (a fact) |
| 认识 | rènshi | to know (a person) |

# Aspect, Not Tense — 了, 过, 在/正在, 着 <!-- slug: aspect-particles -->

*(Language-specific chapter: Mandarin marks HOW an action relates to completion or duration, not WHEN it happened.)*

Chinese doesn't conjugate for tense the way European languages do. Instead, **aspect particles** tell you about the *shape* of the action — completed, ever-experienced, ongoing, or continuing.

## 了 (le) — completion & change of state <!-- slug: aspect-le -->

**了** has two distinct jobs. Getting them mixed up is one of the most common intermediate-level errors.

**Job 1 — verbal 了**: goes directly after the verb, signals the **action is completed**:
- 我吃了饭。(*Wǒ chī le fàn.*) — I ate / I have eaten (the meal is done)
- 他买了一本书。(*Tā mǎi le yī běn shū.*) — He bought a book.

**Job 2 — modal/sentence-final 了**: goes at the END of the sentence, signals a **new situation or change of state**:
- 下雨了。(*Xià yǔ le.*) — It's started raining. (change: rain began)
- 我饿了。(*Wǒ è le.*) — I'm hungry now. (I wasn't before — change)
- 我不去了。(*Wǒ bù qù le.*) — I'm not going anymore. (changed my mind)

Both 了s can appear together: 我吃了饭了 — I've already eaten (the first marks completion, the second marks the current state of having eaten).

✗ Don't equate 了 with "past tense" — it can appear in future contexts too: *你吃了再走。* (Eat first, then leave.)

## 过 (guo) — experienced ever <!-- slug: aspect-guo -->

Marks that something was **ever experienced** at some point in one's life — not about when, just whether:
- 你去过中国吗？(*Nǐ qù guo Zhōngguó ma?*) — Have you ever been to China?
- 我没去过。(*Wǒ méi qù guo.*) — I've never been.
- 我吃过北京烤鸭。(*Wǒ chī guo Běijīng kǎoyā.*) — I've (had the experience of) eaten Peking Duck.

## 在/正在 and 着 (zhe) — ongoing and durative <!-- slug: aspect-ongoing -->

**在/正在** before the verb = action is **in progress right now** (like English "-ing"):
- 我在看书。(*Wǒ zài kàn shū.*) — I'm reading.
- 她正在睡觉。(*Tā zhèngzài shuìjiào.*) — She's sleeping (right now, emphatic).

**着 (zhe)** after the verb = a **continuing state** (the result of an action persists):
- 门开着。(*Mén kāi zhe.*) — The door is open. (opened, and remains open)
- 他站着说话。(*Tā zhàn zhe shuōhuà.*) — He's talking while standing. (maintains standing posture)
- 墙上挂着一幅画。(*Qiáng shàng guà zhe yī fú huà.*) — A picture is hanging on the wall.

# Negation — 不 vs 没(有) <!-- slug: negation -->

Two negators, and the split is crucial:

## 不 (bù) — general, habitual, future, will <!-- slug: negation-bu -->

**不** negates **general facts, habitual actions, present states, future intentions, and adjectives/modal verbs**:
- 我不喝酒。(*Wǒ bù hē jiǔ.*) — I don't drink alcohol. (general/habitual)
- 我不去。(*Wǒ bù qù.*) — I'm not going. (future intention)
- 他不高。(*Tā bù gāo.*) — He's not tall. (adjective/state)
- 我不想吃。(*Wǒ bù xiǎng chī.*) — I don't want to eat.

**不** also negates 是 (to be): *我不是学生。* (I'm not a student.)

## 没(有) (méi/méiyǒu) — past actions, have/haven't <!-- slug: negation-mei -->

**没(有)** negates **completed actions** (the negative counterpart of verbal 了) and **possession** (negates 有):
- 我没吃饭。(*Wǒ méi chī fàn.*) — I didn't eat. / I haven't eaten.
- 他没来。(*Tā méi lái.*) — He didn't come.
- 我没有车。(*Wǒ méi yǒu chē.*) — I don't have a car.

**Critical contrast:**
- *我不吃肉。* — I don't eat meat (general — vegetarian, lifestyle)
- *我没吃肉。* — I didn't eat meat (yesterday, on that occasion)

✗ *我不吃了饭* — wrong. 了 marks completion; negate completion with 没, not 不.
✓ *我没吃饭。*

# Questions <!-- slug: questions -->

## 吗 (ma) — yes/no questions <!-- slug: question-ma -->

Add 吗 to the end of any statement to make it a yes/no question. Word order **does not change** — no inversion:
- 你是学生。→ 你是学生**吗**？(*Nǐ shì xuésheng ma?*) — Are you a student?
- 他来了**吗**？— Has he come?

Answer with the verb, not "yes/no":
- 是 (*shì*) / 不是 (*bú shì*) — yes (it is) / no (it's not)
- 来了 / 没来 — yes (came) / no (didn't come)

## 呢 (ne) — "what about…?" <!-- slug: question-ne -->

Lightweight particle for follow-up questions, meaning "and you? / what about X?":
- *我很好，你**呢**？* — I'm fine, and you?
- *他去了，她**呢**？* — He went, what about her?

## A-not-A questions <!-- slug: question-a-not-a -->

Repeat the verb in positive-negative form — a question without a question word or particle:
- 你去**不去**？(*Nǐ qù bu qù?*) — Are you going (or not)?
- 他是**不是**学生？(*Tā shì bu shì xuésheng?*) — Is he a student?
- 你喜欢**不喜欢**？(*Nǐ xǐhuan bu xǐhuan?*) — Do you like it?

With 有: *你有**没有**时间？* — Do you have time?

## Question words stay in place <!-- slug: question-words-in-place -->

**This is a major structural difference from English.** In Mandarin, question words sit in the position that answers them — the sentence order doesn't change:

| Question | Pinyin | Literal | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| 你叫**什么**名字？ | Nǐ jiào **shénme** míngzi? | You called what name? | What's your name? |
| **谁**来了？ | **Shéi** lái le? | Who came? | Who came? |
| 你去**哪儿**？ | Nǐ qù **nǎr**? | You go where? | Where are you going? |
| 你**为什么**不吃？ | Nǐ **wèishénme** bù chī? | You why not eat? | Why aren't you eating? |
| 这个**多少钱**？ | Zhège **duōshao qián**? | This how much money? | How much is this? |
| **什么时候**去？ | **Shénme shíhou** qù? | What time go? | When are we going? |

✓ 好吗？(*hǎo ma?*) — OK? / all right?
✓ 对吧？(*duì ba?*) — right? / isn't it? (seeking confirmation, like a tag question)
✓ 是吗？(*shì ma?*) — really? (surprised)

# Adjectives as Predicates <!-- slug: adjectives-predicates -->

## No 是 with adjectives — a classic trap <!-- slug: adjectives-no-shi -->

In English: "He is tall." In Chinese you do NOT say *他是高* — **是** is not used with adjectives as predicates. The adjective itself serves as the predicate verb:

- 他很高。(*Tā hěn gāo.*) — He is tall.
- 这本书很好。(*Zhè běn shū hěn hǎo.*) — This book is good.

The **很** (*hěn*, "very") is almost always present even when you don't mean "very" — without it, the sentence sounds like a **comparison** or is pragmatically incomplete. Think of 很 as a grammatical buffer rather than a true intensifier here.

✗ 他高。— grammatically implies "he is taller (than something)" or sounds like a list entry
✓ 他很高。— natural statement "he's tall"

For genuine "very": double it or use 非常 (*fēicháng*, extremely):
- 她非常漂亮。(*Tā fēicháng piàoliang.*) — She's extremely beautiful.

## 是...的 construction <!-- slug: shi-de-construction -->

This structure focuses on the **circumstances of a completed action** — when, where, how, by whom:
- 他是**昨天来**的。(*Tā shì zuótiān lái de.*) — He came **yesterday**. (emphasis: it was yesterday)
- 我是**在北京**学的中文。(*Wǒ shì zài Běijīng xué de Zhōngwén.*) — I learned Chinese **in Beijing**.
- 这个是**谁做**的？(*Zhège shì shéi zuò de?*) — Who made this?

It's about a past fact already known — you're zooming in on the *details* of how it happened.

# Comparisons with 比 <!-- slug: comparisons-bi -->

The standard comparison structure uses **比** (bǐ — compared to):

**A 比 B + adjective** = A is [adj]-er than B

- 他比我高。(*Tā bǐ wǒ gāo.*) — He's taller than me.
- 北京比上海冷。(*Běijīng bǐ Shànghǎi lěng.*) — Beijing is colder than Shanghai.
- 今天比昨天热多了。(*Jīntiān bǐ zuótiān rè duō le.*) — Today is much hotter than yesterday. (多了 = by a lot)

✗ Don't add 更 (*gèng*, "even more") in a basic 比 sentence — the adjective alone carries the comparative meaning.
✗ *他比我更高* — technically possible but overloaded; stick with *他比我高*.

**Equality — 跟...一样 (gēn...yīyàng):**
- 他跟我一样高。(*Tā gēn wǒ yīyàng gāo.*) — He's as tall as me.

**Superlative — 最 (zuì):**
- 她是班里最高的。(*Tā shì bān lǐ zuì gāo de.*) — She's the tallest in the class.

# Coverbs / Prepositions <!-- slug: coverbs-prepositions -->

Mandarin "prepositions" are better called **coverbs** — they are historically verbs that now function prepositionally. They come before the main verb and introduce the context of the action.

| Coverb | Pinyin | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 在 | zài | location of action | 我在家工作。— I work at home. |
| 给 | gěi | for / give to (beneficiary) | 我给你买了礼物。— I bought you a gift. |
| 跟 | gēn | with (accompanying) | 我跟他一起去。— I'm going with him. |
| 从 | cóng | from (starting point) | 他从北京来。— He comes from Beijing. |
| 到 | dào | to / arrive at | 我到学校了。— I've arrived at school. |
| 对 | duì | toward / regarding | 她对我很好。— She's very good to me. |
| 离 | lí | distance from | 图书馆离这儿不远。— The library isn't far from here. |
| 用 | yòng | using / by means of | 你用筷子吃吗？— Do you eat with chopsticks? |
| 向/往 | xiàng/wǎng | toward (direction) | 向右走。— Walk to the right. |
| 为 | wèi | for the sake of | 我为你高兴。— I'm happy for you. |

# Modal / Auxiliary Verbs <!-- slug: modal-verbs -->

These go before the main verb and never change form.

## Ability and possibility <!-- slug: modal-ability -->

| Word | Pinyin | Meaning | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 会 | huì | can (learned skill) / will likely | 我会游泳。— I can swim. |
| 能 | néng | can (circumstantially able) | 我今天不能来。— I can't come today. |
| 可以 | kěyǐ | may / permitted to | 我可以进来吗？— May I come in? |

**会 vs 能**: 会 = acquired skill ("I know how to"), 能 = conditions allow ("I'm able to right now"). A broken leg: *我不能走* (I can't walk — capacity gone), not *我不会走* (which would mean I never learned to walk).

## Desire and obligation <!-- slug: modal-desire-obligation -->

| Word | Pinyin | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 想 | xiǎng | want to / think about | 我想喝水。— I want some water. |
| 要 | yào | want / need / will (future) | 我要一杯咖啡。— I'd like a coffee. |
| 应该 | yīnggāi | should / ought to | 你应该多休息。— You should rest more. |
| 得 | děi | must (necessity) | 我得走了。— I have to go now. |
| 必须 | bìxū | must (strong obligation) | 你必须来。— You must come. |

**要 is multipurpose** — it can mean "want," "need," "going to," or signal future: *要下雨了* — it's going to rain.

# The 把 Construction <!-- slug: ba-construction -->

*(Language-specific chapter: notoriously tricky — the "disposal" construction.)*

**把** (bǎ) moves the object to a **pre-verbal position** to emphasize what happens *to* it. Think of it as saying: "take X and do something with it."

**Structure: Subject + 把 + Object + Verb + Result/Complement**

- 我把书放在桌子上。(*Wǒ bǎ shū fàng zài zhuōzi shàng.*) — I put the book on the table.
- 她把作业做完了。(*Tā bǎ zuòyè zuò wán le.*) — She finished the homework. (did it to completion)
- 他把我的名字写错了。(*Tā bǎ wǒ de míngzi xiě cuò le.*) — He wrote my name wrong.

## When to use 把 <!-- slug: ba-when -->

Use it when:
1. You're emphasizing what is **done to** a specific, definite object
2. The verb has a **result or direction** attached (a complement)
3. The object is somehow **affected, moved, or disposed of**

✗ *我把书看。* — wrong. The verb needs a result complement. Can't just say "I 把-ed the book looked."
✓ *我把书看完了。* — I finished reading the book.

✗ *我把一本书买了。* — doesn't work well. 把 objects must be **definite** (known, specific). Use: *我买了一本书。*
✓ *我把那本书买了。* — I bought that book (the specific one).

**Negation and modals go BEFORE 把:**
- 我没把作业做完。— I didn't finish the homework.
- 你应该把手机关掉。— You should turn off your phone.

# Complex Sentences <!-- slug: complex-sentences -->

## Because...therefore: 因为...所以 <!-- slug: complex-yinwei-suoyi -->

Chinese often states both halves of a causal relationship explicitly — using both 因为 AND 所以 in a single sentence (which feels redundant to English speakers, but is natural in Chinese):

- 因为下雨，所以我没去。(*Yīnwèi xià yǔ, suǒyǐ wǒ méi qù.*) — Because it rained, (so) I didn't go.
- 因为他工作很忙，所以没时间。— Because he's busy with work, (so) he has no time.

One can be dropped in informal speech, but using both is always correct.

## Although...but: 虽然...但是 <!-- slug: complex-suiran-danshi -->

Same paired structure — use both halves:
- 虽然贵，但是很好。(*Suīrán guì, dànshì hěn hǎo.*) — Although it's expensive, it's very good.
- 虽然他很累，但是还在工作。— Although he's tired, he's still working.

✗ Don't use 但是 without 虽然 if you're using the paired structure — they go together.
Note: English says "although...but" is redundant; Chinese requires it.

## 一边...一边 — doing two things simultaneously <!-- slug: complex-yibian -->

- 他一边听音乐，一边写作业。(*Tā yībiān tīng yīnyuè, yībiān xiě zuòyè.*) — He listens to music while doing homework.
- 我们一边喝茶，一边聊天。— We chat while drinking tea.

# Conditionals — 如果/要是...就 <!-- slug: conditionals -->

The standard conditional pattern:

**如果/要是 + condition clause + 就 + result clause**

- 如果明天下雨，我就不去了。(*Rúguǒ míngtiān xià yǔ, wǒ jiù bù qù le.*) — If it rains tomorrow, I won't go.
- 要是你有时间，就来找我。(*Yàoshi nǐ yǒu shíjiān, jiù lái zhǎo wǒ.*) — If you have time, come find me.
- 如果你不学，就不会说中文。— If you don't study, you won't be able to speak Chinese.

**就** (jiù) in the result clause is like a logical "then" — it's often optional but makes the sentence flow naturally. Without a conditional opener, 就 alone signals immediacy or inevitability:
- 我现在就去。— I'll go right now.

**Unreal conditions** use the same structure — context makes it clear:
- 如果我有很多钱，就买一栋大房子。— If I had a lot of money, I'd buy a big house.

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

Plot is typically narrated in **present tense** (no aspect particles, just bare verbs — or 在 for ongoing scenes).

**Basic description structure:**

1. **Genre and basics:**
   - 这是一部**爱情片/动作片/恐怖片/科幻片/纪录片**。(*Zhè shì yī bù àiqíngpiān / dòngzuòpiān / kǒngbùpiān / kēhuànpiān / jìlùpiān.*) — This is a romance/action/horror/sci-fi/documentary.
   - 这部电影是**XX导演**拍的。(*Zhè bù diànyǐng shì XX dǎoyǎn pāi de.*) — This film was directed by XX.
   - 故事发生在... (*Gùshi fāshēng zài...*) — The story takes place in...

2. **Plot:**
   - 这部电影讲的是... (*Zhè bù diànyǐng jiǎng de shì...*) — This film is about...
   - 主人公是一个... (*Zhǔréngōng shì yī gè...*) — The main character is a...
   - 最后... (*Zuìhòu...*) — In the end...

3. **Opinion:**
   - 我觉得这部电影很好看。(*Wǒ juéde zhè bù diànyǐng hěn hǎokàn.*) — I think this film is great.
   - 演员演得很好。(*Yǎnyuán yǎn de hěn hǎo.*) — The acting is very good. (结果补语 structure: V + 得 + assessment)
   - 剧情很精彩/很无聊。(*Jùqíng hěn jīngcǎi / hěn wúliáo.*) — The plot is fantastic / boring.
   - 结局很出乎意料。(*Jiéjú hěn chū hū yìliào.*) — The ending is very unexpected.
   - 我强烈推荐！(*Wǒ qiángliè tuījiàn!*) — I strongly recommend it!
   - 值得一看。(*Zhídé yī kàn.*) — Worth watching.

**Vocabulary:**
- 电影 (*diànyǐng*) — film/movie; 电视剧 (*diànshìjù*) — TV series
- 导演 (*dǎoyǎn*) — director; 演员 (*yǎnyuán*) — actor/actress
- 主角/主人公 (*zhǔjué/zhǔréngōng*) — main character; 反派 (*fǎnpài*) — villain
- 情节/剧情 (*qíngjié/jùqíng*) — plot; 结局 (*jiéjú*) — ending
- 特效 (*tèxiào*) — special effects; 配乐 (*pèiyuè*) — soundtrack
- 感人 (*gǎnrén*) — moving/touching; 刺激 (*cìjī*) — thrilling; 搞笑 (*gǎoxiào*) — funny
