# The Three Writing Systems <!-- slug: writing-systems -->

Japanese uses **three scripts simultaneously** — sometimes in a single sentence. This is the first thing to understand, because it explains why Japanese looks chaotic at first and highly structured once you see the logic.

## Hiragana (ひらがな) <!-- slug: hiragana -->

46 syllable symbols. **All native Japanese grammar is written in hiragana** — verb endings, particles, conjunctions, grammatical words. You must learn hiragana first; it's the skeleton of the language. Learnable in a week with daily practice.

Each symbol represents a consonant+vowel mora (or a bare vowel): *か (ka), き (ki), く (ku), け (ke), こ (ko)*. The vowels are pure: **a** (as in *father*), **i** (as in *bee*), **u** (as in *blue* — unrounded), **e** (as in *bet*), **o** (as in *more*).

## Katakana (カタカナ) <!-- slug: katakana -->

46 symbols, same sounds as hiragana, different shapes. **Katakana is used for foreign loanwords (gairaigo), foreign names, and emphasis**. If you see angular, sharp-cornered characters, it's katakana.

*テレビ (terebi — TV), コーヒー (kōhī — coffee), アメリカ (Amerika — America), スマホ (sumaho — smartphone)*

The long vowel mark ー (chōonpu) extends the preceding vowel: *コーヒー* has two long vowels.

## Kanji (漢字) <!-- slug: kanji -->

Chinese-derived logographic characters. Each has at least two readings: **on-yomi** (Chinese-derived, used in compound words) and **kun-yomi** (Japanese native reading, used standalone or with kana endings).

*山 (yama / san — mountain), 水 (mizu / sui — water), 人 (hito / jin/nin — person)*

There are ~2,000 characters in the standard joyo kanji set (taught through high school). You don't need all of them to have real conversations, but reading anything authentic requires building a kanji base gradually.

## How they combine

A single sentence uses all three:
*私はコーヒーを飲みます。* (Watashi wa kōhī o nomimasu.) — I drink coffee.

- 私 = kanji (watashi — I)
- は = hiragana (wa — topic particle)
- コーヒー = katakana (kōhī — coffee, loanword)
- を = hiragana (o — object particle)
- 飲みます = kanji + hiragana (nomi-masu — drink [polite])

Practical advice: learn hiragana and katakana before anything else. Apps like Anki with mnemonics get most learners through both in 2–3 weeks. Kanji is a long game — start with the most frequent 100 and grow from there.

# Pronunciation <!-- slug: pronunciation -->

## Mora timing

Japanese is **mora-timed**, not syllable-timed. Each mora takes approximately equal time. This means *さっき (sakki — a moment ago)* has 3 morae (sa-k-ki) not 2 syllables — the double consonant (っ/ッ) is a full mora of silence before the consonant.

Long vowels also count as 2 morae: *おかあさん (okāsan — mother)* = o-ka-a-sa-n = 5 morae. Shortening long vowels changes or destroys meaning.

## Particles as pronounced

Three hiragana have unexpected readings as particles:
- **は** (ha) → read as **wa** when used as topic particle
- **を** (wo) → read as **o** when used as object particle
- **へ** (he) → read as **e** when used as direction particle

## Pitch accent

Japanese has **pitch accent** — words are distinguished by a pattern of high (H) and low (L) tones, not by stress as in English. The exact pattern varies by region (Tokyo dialect is standard).

*橋 hashi* (bridge) H-L vs *箸 hashi* (chopsticks) L-H — same romanization, different meaning, different pitch.

For beginners: pitch accent won't prevent communication, but listening carefully to native speakers and mimicking their intonation is more important than in English.

## Consonant notes

- **r** (ら り る れ ろ) — a light tap, between English r and l. Not a rolled r, not an English r. Your tongue taps once behind the upper teeth, like a very light d.
- **fu** (ふ) — a bilabial fricative, not quite English "f" (which uses teeth). Lips barely touch, breath passes through.
- Double consonants (っ) — a held beat of silence: *きって (kitte — stamp)* vs *きて (kite — come [te-form])* — these are different words.

# Basic Sentence Structure & Core Particles <!-- slug: particles -->

Particles are the most important thing in Japanese grammar. They are **postpositions** — they follow the word they mark, opposite to English prepositions. Particles do what word order and prepositions do in English.

Japanese default word order is **SOV: Subject – Object – Verb**. The verb goes at the end. Always.

*私は本を読みます。* (Watashi **wa** hon **o** yomimasu.) — I read books. (lit: I-TOPIC book-OBJECT read-POLITE)

You can rearrange most elements and it remains grammatical (the particles carry the meaning), but the verb stays last.

## は vs が — the famous distinction <!-- slug: wa-vs-ga -->

This is the distinction that trips up every Japanese learner for months. Both relate to the subject — but they do completely different things.

**は (wa) — Topic Marker**

は marks what the sentence is **about**. It establishes the topic of conversation. It can mark subjects, objects, time expressions — any element elevated to "topic." It often implies contrast.

**が (ga) — Subject Marker**

が marks the **grammatical subject** — the agent of the verb. It highlights the *identity* of who does something, often in answer to "who?" It does not imply contrast.

The difference in practice:

| Japanese | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 私**は**学生です。 (Watashi wa gakusei desu.) | **As for me**, I am a student. (introducing myself, topic) |
| 私**が**学生です。 (Watashi ga gakusei desu.) | **I** am the student. (not someone else — identifying) |
| 猫**は**魚を食べます。 (Neko wa sakana o tabemasu.) | **As for cats**, they eat fish. (general statement about cats) |
| 猫**が**魚を食べました。 (Neko ga sakana o tabemashita.) | **The cat** ate the fish. (identifying: it was the cat, not the dog) |

More rules:
- New information is marked with が: *あ、猫がいる！ (A, neko ga iru!) — Oh, there's a cat!*
- Known/previously mentioned topic switches to は: *猫はかわいいです。 (The cat is cute.)*
- Certain verbs always take が for their subject: *わかる (wakaru — understand), できる (dekiru — can do), ある/いる (there is), 好き (suki — like), 欲しい (hoshii — want)*: *コーヒーが好きです。 (I like coffee.)* ✗ ~~コーヒーをすきです~~
- は in negative/contrast sentences: *魚**は**食べません。* (Fish, I don't eat — implying I eat other things.)

Mnemonic: **は = As for…** / **が = IT IS … (who/what)**. Read every は sentence as "As for [topic]…"

## を (o) — Object Marker

Marks the **direct object** of transitive verbs. Straightforward:
*本を読む (hon o yomu — read a book) | 水を飲む (mizu o nomu — drink water) | 音楽を聞く (ongaku o kiku — listen to music)*

Also marks the space traversed with motion verbs: *公園を歩く (kōen o aruku — walk through the park)*

## に (ni) — Time / Destination / Indirect Object <!-- slug: ni-particle -->

に is the workhorse particle with three major uses:

**1. Specific point in time:**
*三時に (san-ji ni — at 3 o'clock) | 月曜日に (getsuyōbi ni — on Monday) | 2024年に (2024-nen ni — in 2024)*
⚠️ No に with relative time words: *今日 (kyō — today), 明日 (ashita — tomorrow), 毎日 (mainichi — every day)* — no particle.

**2. Destination of movement:**
*東京に行く (Tōkyō ni iku — go to Tokyo) | 家に帰る (ie ni kaeru — return home) | 学校に来る (gakkō ni kuru — come to school)*

**3. Indirect object (recipient):**
*友達に電話する (tomodachi ni denwa suru — call a friend) | 先生に聞く (sensei ni kiku — ask the teacher) | 彼女に手紙を書く (kanojo ni tegami o kaku — write a letter to her)*

**4. Location of existence (with ある/いる):**
*駅に自販機がある。 (Eki ni jidōhanbaiki ga aru.) — There is a vending machine at the station.*

## へ (e) — Direction

Marks direction of movement. Similar to に for movement, but emphasizes the **direction** rather than the destination:
*東へ行く (higashi e iku — go toward the east)*
In daily speech, に and へ are often interchangeable for movement.

## で (de) — Location of Action / Means <!-- slug: de-particle -->

**Location where an action takes place** (contrast with に which marks existence/destination):
*図書館で勉強する (toshokan de benkyō suru — study at the library)*
*レストランで食べる (resutoran de taberu — eat at a restaurant)*

**Means or method:**
*電車で行く (densha de iku — go by train) | 日本語で話す (nihongo de hanasu — speak in Japanese) | 箸で食べる (hashi de taberu — eat with chopsticks)*

**で vs に location distinction:**
- *図書館**に**います。 (Toshokan ni imasu.) — I am at the library.* (existence — に)
- *図書館**で**勉強します。 (Toshokan de benkyō shimasu.) — I study at the library.* (action — で)

## と (to) — With / And (exhaustive list)

*友達と行く (tomodachi to iku — go with a friend)*
*りんごとみかんを買った (ringo to mikan o katta — bought apples AND oranges)* — と lists exhaustively (these things, no others). For non-exhaustive "and" between nouns: use や.

## から / まで (kara / made) — From / Until

*東京から大阪まで (Tōkyō kara Ōsaka made — from Tokyo to Osaka)*
*9時から5時まで働く (ku-ji kara go-ji made hataraku — work from 9 to 5)*

## の (no) — Possessive / Linking

*私の本 (watashi no hon — my book) | 日本語の先生 (nihongo no sensei — Japanese teacher)*
の can chain: *私の大学の友達 (my university friend)*
Also makes a noun out of a clause: *好きなの (sukina no — the one I like)*

## も (mo) — Also / Even

Replaces は or が (never stacks): *私も行きます。 (Watashi mo ikimasu.) — I'm going too.*
With negatives: *私も行きません。 — I'm not going either.*

## か (ka) — Question Marker

Turns a sentence into a yes/no question (polite speech — see Questions chapter).

# Nouns, Counters & There-Is <!-- slug: nouns-counters -->

## No plural, no gender, no articles

Japanese nouns do not change form. *猫 (neko)* can mean "a cat," "the cat," "cats," or "the cats" — context determines which. There is no grammatical gender.

*犬が好きです。 (Inu ga suki desu.)* — I like dogs / I like a dog / I like the dog.

## Counters (助数詞 josūshi)

Japanese cannot attach bare numbers to nouns — a **counter word** is always needed. Each category of thing has its own counter:

| Counter | Reading | Used for | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 人 | -ri (1,2), -nin (3+) | people | 一人 (hitori), 二人 (futari), 三人 (san-nin) |
| 個 | -ko | small objects | りんご三個 (ringo san-ko — 3 apples) |
| 枚 | -mai | flat things | 紙一枚 (kami ichi-mai — 1 sheet of paper) |
| 本 | -hon/-pon/-bon | long thin things | ペン二本 (pen ni-hon — 2 pens) |
| 冊 | -satsu | bound books | 本三冊 (hon san-satsu — 3 books) |
| 台 | -dai | machines/vehicles | 車一台 (kuruma ichi-dai — 1 car) |
| 匹 | -hiki/-piki | small animals | 猫二匹 (neko ni-hiki — 2 cats) |
| 頭 | -tō | large animals | 牛三頭 (ushi san-tō — 3 cows) |
| 杯 | -hai/-pai/-bai | cups/bowls | コーヒー一杯 (kōhī ippai — 1 cup of coffee) |
| つ | -tsu | general (1-9 only) | 一つ、二つ、三つ… (hitotsu, futatsu, mittsu…) |

The つ counter is native Japanese (not Sino-Japanese) and works as a default for miscellaneous objects up to 9. After 10, use specific counters or 個.

Note: reading changes happen (hon → pon/bon, hiki → piki/biki) depending on the preceding number — these are rendaku (sequential voicing) and must be memorized per counter. Learning the top 6–8 counters covers most daily needs.

## There-is: あります vs います <!-- slug: arimasu-imasu -->

Two verbs for "there is / to exist" — choosing wrong marks you as a beginner:

- **あります (arimasu)** — for inanimate things (objects, plants)
- **います (imasu)** — for animate beings (people, animals)

*机の上に本があります。 (Tsukue no ue ni hon ga arimasu.)* — There is a book on the desk.
*公園に子供がいます。 (Kōen ni kodomo ga imasu.)* — There are children in the park.
*ここに猫がいます。 (Koko ni neko ga imasu.)* — There is a cat here. (cat = animate → います)

Negative: *ありません (arimasen) / いません (imasen)*
Plain form: *ある / いる*

# Pronouns & こそあど Demonstratives <!-- slug: pronouns-demonstratives -->

## Personal pronouns

Japanese has many "I" and "you" words, all carrying social nuance:

| Meaning | Word | Register |
|---|---|---|
| I | 私 (watashi) | neutral, polite |
| I | 僕 (boku) | masculine, informal |
| I | 俺 (ore) | masculine, rough |
| I | あたし (atashi) | feminine, casual |
| you | あなた (anata) | polite but often avoided |
| you | 君 (kimi) | informal, male speech |
| you | お前 (omae) | rough, can be rude |

In practice, **Japanese speakers often omit pronouns entirely** — subject and object are inferred from context. Saying あなた to someone you know their name sounds oddly distant; use their name or title instead.

Third person: 彼 (kare — he), 彼女 (kanojo — she). These also mean "boyfriend/girlfriend" — context matters.

## こそあど (ko-so-a-do) — the demonstrative system <!-- slug: kosoado -->

Japanese organizes demonstratives into a beautifully regular 4-way system based on distance:

| | Near speaker (ko-) | Near listener (so-) | Far from both (a-) | Unknown/question (do-) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| thing | これ (kore — this) | それ (sore — that) | あれ (are — that over there) | どれ (dore — which) |
| place | ここ (koko — here) | そこ (soko — there) | あそこ (asoko — over there) | どこ (doko — where) |
| direction | こちら (kochira) | そちら (sochira) | あちら (achira) | どちら (dochira) |
| adj/noun modifier | この (kono — this N) | その (sono — that N) | あの (ano — that N over there) | どの (dono — which N) |
| manner | こう (kō — this way) | そう (sō — that way) | ああ (ā — that way) | どう (dō — how) |

*これは何ですか？ (Kore wa nan desu ka?) — What is this?*
*あの映画が好きです。 (Ano eiga ga suki desu.) — I like that movie (over there / we both know which one).*

あの in conversation often refers to something both speakers know: *あの映画* = "that movie [we both know about]."
そう (sō) is behind the ubiquitous response *そうですか (Sō desu ka?) — Is that so? / I see.*

# Questions <!-- slug: questions -->

## Yes/no questions

In **polite speech**, add **か (ka)** to the end of a statement:
*これは本ですか？ (Kore wa hon desu ka?) — Is this a book?*
*日本語を話しますか？ (Nihongo o hanashimasu ka?) — Do you speak Japanese?*

In **casual speech**, か alone can sound harsh (masculine/rough). Rising intonation alone works: *これ本？ (Kore, hon?) — This, a book?*

Answering:
- *はい (hai) — yes* / *いいえ (iie) — no*
- Softened yes: *そうです (sō desu) — that's right* | Softened no: *ちょっと… (chotto… — a little… [politely declining])*

In Japanese, yes/no answers confirm or deny the **fact**, not the grammar. *日本語を話しませんか？ (Don't you speak Japanese?) → はい、話しません (Yes, I don't speak it) / いいえ、話します (No, I do speak it).* — The reverse of English!

## Question words

| Japanese | Rōmaji | English |
|---|---|---|
| 何 | nani / nan | what |
| 誰 | dare | who |
| どこ | doko | where |
| いつ | itsu | when |
| なぜ / どうして | naze / dōshite | why |
| どう | dō | how |
| どんな | donna | what kind of |
| いくつ | ikutsu | how many (things) |
| いくら | ikura | how much (price) |
| どのくらい | dono kurai | how long/far |

Question words go where the answer would be — no inversion, no do-support:
*彼は何を食べますか？ (Kare wa nani o tabemasu ka?) — What does he eat?*
*あなたはどこに住んでいますか？ (Anata wa doko ni sunde imasu ka?) — Where do you live?*

# Verbs — Three Groups <!-- slug: verb-groups -->

Japanese verbs inflect heavily, but the system is highly regular once you know which of three groups a verb belongs to.

## Group 1 — Godan verbs (五段動詞) <!-- slug: godan -->

"5-step" verbs. Dictionary form ends in any う-row syllable: *ku, gu, su, tsu, nu, bu, mu, ru, u*. The final syllable "steps" through 5 rows of the kana table to make different forms.

Common examples: *書く (kaku — write), 話す (hanasu — speak), 飲む (nomu — drink), 読む (yomu — read), 行く (iku — go), 聞く (kiku — listen), 買う (kau — buy), 待つ (matsu — wait), 乗る (noru — ride)*

## Group 2 — Ichidan verbs (一段動詞) <!-- slug: ichidan -->

"1-step" verbs. Dictionary form always ends in **-ru**, with **-i** or **-e** before the -ru. Only one change needed — just drop the -ru.

Common examples: *食べる (taberu — eat), 見る (miru — see), 起きる (okiru — wake up), 寝る (neru — sleep), 着る (kiru — wear), 教える (oshieru — teach), 開ける (akeru — open)*

⚠️ Trap: some -ru verbs are actually Godan: *帰る (kaeru — return home), 走る (hashiru — run), 切る (kiru — cut), 入る (hairu — enter)*. These must be memorized.

Mnemonic: if there's an **i** or **e** sound directly before -ru, it's probably Ichidan. But always check for the exceptions.

## Group 3 — Irregular (不規則動詞) <!-- slug: irregular-verbs -->

Only **two**: *する (suru — do)* and *来る (kuru — come)*. Memorize their conjugations — they appear constantly.

## The polite ます-form

All three groups produce the polite ます form the same final way — attach ます to the verb stem. The stem is formed differently per group:

| Group | Dictionary | Stem | ます form |
|---|---|---|---|
| Godan (kaku) | 書く | 書き (kaki) | 書きます (kakimasu) |
| Godan (nomu) | 飲む | 飲み (nomi) | 飲みます (nomimasu) |
| Ichidan (taberu) | 食べる | 食べ (tabe) | 食べます (tabemasu) |
| Irregular (suru) | する | し (shi) | します (shimasu) |
| Irregular (kuru) | 来る | き (ki) | 来ます (kimasu) |

For Godan: the final う-row syllable shifts to its い-row counterpart: く→き, す→し, む→み, ぬ→に, ぶ→び, つ→ち, る→り, う→い, ぐ→ぎ.

ます conjugates: *ます (non-past positive) / ません (non-past negative) / ました (past positive) / ませんでした (past negative)*

# Verb Forms & "Tense" <!-- slug: verb-tense -->

Japanese has **two tenses** (non-past and past) and two aspects (ongoing vs complete), not the elaborate tense system of European languages.

## Non-past vs Past

**Non-past** covers both present and future. Context (time words, particles) distinguishes:
*毎日コーヒーを飲みます。 (Mainichi kōhī o nomimasu.)* — I drink coffee every day. (habit)
*明日友達に会います。 (Ashita tomodachi ni aimasu.)* — I'll meet my friend tomorrow. (future)

**Past (た-form)**: dictionary form → た-form. The suffix depends on the verb group:

| Godan ending | Past suffix | Example |
|---|---|---|
| く | いた | 書く → 書いた (kaita — wrote) |
| ぐ | いだ | 泳ぐ → 泳いだ (oyoida — swam) |
| す | した | 話す → 話した (hanashita — spoke) |
| む/ぶ/ぬ | んだ | 飲む → 飲んだ (nonda — drank) |
| つ/う/る (godan) | った | 待つ → 待った (matta — waited) |
| る (ichidan) | た | 食べる → 食べた (tabeta — ate) |

Exception: 行く (iku) → 行った (itta — NOT いいた). する → した. 来る → 来た (kita).

## Negative ない-form

Non-past negative: add **ない** to the negative stem.
- Godan: final syllable shifts to あ-row + ない: *書く → 書か**ない**, 飲む → 飲ま**ない***, 行く → 行か**ない**
- Ichidan: drop る + ない: *食べる → 食べ**ない***
- Exception: verbs ending in う → わない: *買う → 買わない (kawanai)*
- する → しない | 来る → 来ない (konai)

Polite negative: ません (as above).

## The て-form — the Swiss army knife <!-- slug: te-form -->

The て-form (te-form) is perhaps the most useful thing to learn in Japanese. It is formed like the past た-form but with て/で instead of た/だ.

Formation: same rules as past, just swap た→て, だ→で:
*書く → 書いて (kaite) | 飲む → 飲んで (nonde) | 食べる → 食べて (tabete) | する → して (shite) | 来る → 来て (kite)*

**Uses of て-form:**

**1. Requests (て + ください):**
*書いてください。 (Kaite kudasai.)* — Please write. | *ちょっと待ってください。 (Chotto matte kudasai.)* — Please wait a moment.
Softer: *〜てくれる？ (〜te kureru?)* — casual request between friends.

**2. Linking actions in sequence:**
*シャワーを浴びて、朝ごはんを食べました。 (Shawā o abite, asagohan o tabemashita.)* — I showered and (then) ate breakfast.
The て-form acts like "and then" — actions unfold in order.

**3. 〜ている (te iru) — progressive / ongoing state:**
This construction covers two things English splits:
- Action in progress now: *本を読んでいます。 (Hon o yonde imasu.)* — I am reading a book.
- Resulting state (from a completed action): *結婚しています。 (Kekkon shite imasu.)* — I am married. (lit: I have married and am in that state) | *窓が開いています。 (Mado ga aite imasu.)* — The window is open. (lit: the window has opened and is in that state)

This second use is critical: many things English describes with adjectives, Japanese describes with 〜ている of a verb.

**4. Asking/giving permission (〜ていい):**
*ここに座ってもいいですか？ (Koko ni suwatte mo ii desu ka?)* — May I sit here? | *食べていいよ。 (Tabete ii yo.)* — You can eat (it's fine).

**5. Prohibition (〜てはいけない):**
*ここで写真を撮ってはいけません。 (Koko de shashin o totte wa ikemasen.)* — You must not take photos here.

# i-Adjectives vs na-Adjectives <!-- slug: adjectives -->

Japanese adjectives come in two grammatically distinct types. Learning which is which determines whether you conjugate them.

## i-Adjectives (い形容詞 i-keiyōshi)

Dictionary form ends in **い** (i). These **conjugate directly** — the い changes:

| Form | Ending | Example (大きい ōkii — big) |
|---|---|---|
| Non-past positive | い | 大きい (big) |
| Non-past negative | くない | 大きくない (not big) |
| Past positive | かった | 大きかった (was big) |
| Past negative | くなかった | 大きくなかった (was not big) |
| Adverb | く | 大きく (largely/bigly) |

*昨日の映画は面白かった。 (Kinō no eiga wa omoshirokatta.)* — Yesterday's movie was interesting.
*このバッグは高くない。 (Kono baggu wa takakunai.)* — This bag is not expensive.

⚠️ Exception: **いい (ii — good)** is irregular — its conjugated forms use **よ**: *よくない (yokunai — not good), よかった (yokatta — was good)*. よかった！ (Yokatta!) = "That's great!" / "What a relief!" — you'll hear this constantly.

## na-Adjectives (な形容詞 na-keiyōshi)

Before a noun, they need **な (na)**. They do NOT directly inflect — they work more like nouns, conjugated through です:

*静かな部屋 (shizuka na heya — quiet room) | 有名な人 (yūmei na hito — famous person)*

Conjugation uses です: *静かです / 静かじゃない / 静かでした / 静かじゃなかった*

Common na-adjectives: *好き (suki — liked/likeable), 嫌い (kirai — disliked), 上手 (jōzu — skillful), 下手 (heta — unskillful), 暇 (hima — free/bored), 大変 (taihen — tough), 有名 (yūmei — famous), 元気 (genki — healthy/energetic), 親切 (shinsetsu — kind)*

✗ Trap: *好き* is a na-adjective, not a verb: *猫が好きです (neko ga suki desu)* = I like cats. NOT *猫を好きです* — use が, not を.

# The Copula です/だ <!-- slug: copula -->

**です (desu)** is the polite copula. **だ (da)** is the plain/casual form.

| Form | Polite | Plain |
|---|---|---|
| Non-past positive | です | だ |
| Non-past negative | じゃないです / ではありません | じゃない / ではない |
| Past positive | でした | だった |
| Past negative | じゃなかったです / ではありませんでした | じゃなかった |

*これはペンです。 (Kore wa pen desu.)* — This is a pen.
*彼女は学生じゃない。 (Kanojo wa gakusei ja nai.)* — She is not a student. (plain)
*昨日は暇でした。 (Kinō wa hima deshita.)* — Yesterday I was free.

One trap: です after i-adjectives is **optional decoration**, not the conjugating element: *大きいです* (polite) vs *大きい* (plain) — the い already carries the tense. ✗ ~~大きかったです~~ is technically allowed in modern usage but purists prefer ~~大きかった~~ in plain speech.

# Sentence-Final Particles ね and よ <!-- slug: ne-yo -->

These tiny particles communicate the speaker's stance and are absolutely central to sounding natural.

## よ (yo) — new information, assertion

The speaker **informs** the listener of something they don't know. It asserts the speaker's knowledge:
*あのレストランはおいしいですよ。 (Ano resutoran wa oishii desu yo.)* — That restaurant is good, you know. (I'm telling you — trust me.)
*明日は雨ですよ。 (Ashita wa ame desu yo.)* — It's going to rain tomorrow. (heads up, you may not know)

## ね (ne) — seeking agreement, shared feeling

The speaker **checks in** with the listener, seeks confirmation, or shares an experience:
*いい天気ですね。 (Ii tenki desu ne.)* — Nice weather, isn't it? (I feel this, do you agree?)
*このケーキはおいしいですね。 (Kono kēki wa oishii desu ne.)* — This cake is delicious, isn't it?

Combined: **よね** = asserting AND seeking confirmation: *明日、会議がありますよね？ (Ashita, kaigi ga arimasu yo ne?)* — There's a meeting tomorrow, right? (I believe so — confirm?)

Overusing よ sounds pushy (you're always correcting people). Overusing ね sounds indecisive. Native speakers calibrate these naturally — start with ね in social contexts.

# Politeness Levels & Keigo <!-- slug: keigo -->

Japanese has a grammatically encoded **politeness system**. This is not just vocabulary choice — different verb forms exist for different social registers.

## The main levels

**Plain/casual (普通体 futsūtai / タメ口 tameguchi):**
Used with close friends, family, people younger/equal in status. Verbs in dictionary/た form, だ copula, no ます:
*どこ行くの？ (Doko iku no?)* — Where are you going?

**Polite (丁寧語 teineigo):**
The default safe register for most situations — strangers, workplaces, service contexts. Uses ます/です:
*どちらへいらっしゃいますか。* — Where are you going? (formal)

**Honorific language (敬語 keigo):**
A full system of **humble speech** (謙譲語 kenjōgo — lowering yourself) and **respectful speech** (尊敬語 sonkeigo — elevating the other person). Used in business, with customers, with superiors.

Key keigo verbs to recognize:

| Plain | Humble (self) | Respectful (other) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| いる | おる | いらっしゃる | to be/exist |
| 行く | 参る (mairu) | いらっしゃる | to go |
| 来る | 参る (mairu) | いらっしゃる | to come |
| 食べる/飲む | いただく | めしあがる | eat/drink |
| 言う | 申す (mōsu) | おっしゃる | to say |
| する | いたす | なさる | to do |
| もらう | いただく | くださる | to receive/give |

*どうぞ召し上がってください。 (Dōzo meshiagatte kudasai.)* — Please go ahead and eat. (respectful — elevating the listener)
*私がいたします。 (Watashi ga itashimasu.)* — I will do it. (humble — lowering self)

For beginners: learn polite ます/です first. Recognize keigo when you hear it. Producing keigo accurately takes years — even native Japanese learn it formally for job interviews.

# Comparisons <!-- slug: comparisons -->

## より (yori) — than

*AはBより〜です。 (A wa B yori ~ desu.)* — A is more ~ than B.

*東京は大阪より大きいです。 (Tōkyō wa Ōsaka yori ōkii desu.)* — Tokyo is bigger than Osaka.
*電車はバスより速い。 (Densha wa basu yori hayai.)* — Trains are faster than buses.

## 〜のほうが (no hō ga) — … is more / the one that is more

*AよりBのほうが〜です。 (A yori B no hō ga ~ desu.)* — B is more ~ than A. (comparing, selecting B as the higher)

*バスより電車のほうが速い。 (Basu yori densha no hō ga hayai.)* — Trains are faster than buses. (lit: trains' side is faster)

Used in preference questions: *コーヒーと紅茶、どちらのほうが好きですか？ (Kōhī to kōcha, dochira no hō ga suki desu ka?)* — Which do you prefer, coffee or tea?

## いちばん (ichiban) — most / number one

*〜の中で、Aがいちばん〜です。 (〜no naka de, A ga ichiban ~ desu.)* — Among ~, A is the most ~.

*クラスの中で、田中さんがいちばん背が高い。 (Kurasu no naka de, Tanaka-san ga ichiban se ga takai.)* — In the class, Tanaka is the tallest.

# The Four Conditionals <!-- slug: conditionals -->

Japanese has four conditional forms where English has roughly two. Each carries a different nuance of conditionality.

## と (to) — natural consequence / universal truth <!-- slug: to-conditional -->

"When X naturally happens, Y follows." Not for requests or willed outcomes.

*右に曲がると、コンビニがあります。 (Migi ni magaru to, konbini ga arimasu.)* — If you turn right, there's a convenience store.
*春になると、桜が咲く。 (Haru ni naru to, sakura ga saku.)* — When spring comes, the cherry blossoms bloom.
*ボタンを押すと、ドアが開きます。 (Botan o osu to, doa ga akimasu.)* — When you press the button, the door opens.

✗ Cannot use と for requests or intentions: ✗ ~~雨が降ると、家にいてください~~ — use たら or ば for that.

## ば (ba) — hypothetical / emphatic condition <!-- slug: ba-conditional -->

Focuses on the condition as a prerequisite. Often carries a nuance of "as long as / provided that." Formed by changing the final う-row vowel to えば:
*書く → 書けば (kakeba), 飲む → 飲めば (nomeba), 食べる → 食べれば (tabereba), する → すれば, 来る → 来れば*

*もっと勉強すれば、合格できる。 (Motto benkyō sureba, gōkaku dekiru.)* — If you study more, you'll pass.
*お金があれば、旅行に行くのに。 (Okane ga areba, ryokō ni iku noni.)* — If I had money, I'd go traveling. (counterfactual, with のに expressing regret)

Often used in set phrases: *よければ (yokereba — if it's okay with you), なければ (nakereba — if there isn't)*

## たら (tara) — upon completion / after / when (specific) <!-- slug: tara-conditional -->

Formed by た-form + ら. Most versatile conditional — use this when unsure.

*家に帰ったら、電話して。 (Ie ni kaettara, denwa shite.)* — When you get home, call me. (action completed → then do Y)
*雨が降ったら、行きません。 (Ame ga futtara, ikimasen.)* — If it rains, I won't go.
*宝くじが当たったら、世界旅行したい。 (Takarakuji ga atattara, sekai ryokō shitai.)* — If I won the lottery, I'd want to travel the world.

たら can express past discovery: *窓を開けたら、猫がいた。 (Mado o aketara, neko ga ita.)* — When I opened the window, there was a cat.

## なら (nara) — given that / if it's the case that <!-- slug: nara-conditional -->

Responds to something just said or assumed. "If what you're saying is true / if that's the situation."

*日本に行くなら、京都に行ってみて。 (Nihon ni iku nara, Kyōto ni itte mite.)* — If you're going to Japan [as you say], try going to Kyoto.
*それが本当なら、大変だ。 (Sore ga hontō nara, taihen da.)* — If that's true, it's serious.
*安いなら、買う。 (Yasui nara, kau.)* — If it's cheap [as you say], I'll buy it.

なら responds to context — it doesn't assert the condition; it **accepts** information just given and draws a conclusion.

**Summary comparison:**

| Conditional | Core feel | Example situation |
|---|---|---|
| と | automatic, natural law | turn the key → engine starts |
| ば | prerequisite/hypothetical | provided you study → you'll pass |
| たら | upon completing / specific if | when you arrive → call me |
| なら | given what you told me | if you're going to Tokyo → visit Shinjuku |

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

**Convention**: narrate plot in **non-past** (present) form — same as English.

**Opening:**
*この映画は〜についての話です。 (Kono eiga wa ~ ni tsuite no hanashi desu.)* — This movie is a story about ~.
*映画の舞台は〜です。 (Eiga no butai wa ~ desu.)* — The setting of the movie is ~.
*主人公は〜という人物です。 (Shujinkō wa ~ to iu jinbutsu desu.)* — The protagonist is a character called ~.

**Genre vocabulary:**
*コメディ (komedi — comedy), サスペンス (saspensu — thriller/suspense), ドラマ (dorama — drama), アクション (akushon — action), ホラー (horā — horror), SF (esu-efu — sci-fi), アニメ (anime — animation), ドキュメンタリー (dokyumentarī — documentary)*

**Plot narration (non-past):**
*主人公は〜しようとしますが、〜という問題が起きます。 (Shujinkō wa ~ shiyō to shimasu ga, ~ to iu mondai ga okimasu.)* — The protagonist tries to ~, but a problem arises where ~.

**Opinion:**
- *面白かったです。 (Omoshirokatta desu.)* — It was interesting. (past i-adjective)
- *感動しました。 (Kandō shimashita.)* — I was moved.
- *ストーリーが予想外でした。 (Sutōrī ga yosōgai deshita.)* — The story was unexpected.
- *おすすめです。 (Osusume desu.)* — I recommend it.
- *ちょっと長すぎました。 (Chotto nagasugimashita.)* — It was a bit too long. (*すぎる sugiru* = too much — attach to adjective stem or verb stem)

**Key vocabulary:**
*監督 (kantoku — director), 俳優 (haiyū — actor), 女優 (joyū — actress), 主人公 (shujinkō — protagonist), 悪役 (akuyaku — villain), セリフ (serifu — dialogue/lines), 字幕 (jimaku — subtitles), 吹き替え (fukikae — dubbing), 結末 (ketsumatsu — ending), 演技 (engi — acting performance)*
