# The Cyrillic Alphabet <!-- slug: alphabet-pronunciation-basics -->

Russian uses the **Cyrillic** script — 33 letters. Spend a week learning to read it and you'll unlock pronunciation, dictionaries, and the feeling of the language immediately. It's faster to learn than it looks.

## The letters — grouped by how they relate to Latin

**Look familiar, sound familiar**:
| Cyrillic | Sound | Example |
|---|---|---|
| А а | "a" | *аптека* (apteka) — pharmacy |
| Е е | "ye" | *есть* (yest') — to eat/there is |
| К к | "k" | *кот* (kot) — cat |
| М м | "m" | *мама* (mama) |
| О о | "o" (stressed); "a" (unstressed) | *окно* (okno) — window |
| Т т | "t" | *там* (tam) — there |

**Look familiar, sound different (the traps)**:
| Cyrillic | Sound | Do NOT confuse with |
|---|---|---|
| В в | "v" | looks like B |
| Н н | "n" | looks like H |
| Р р | rolled "r" | looks like P |
| С с | "s" | looks like C |
| У у | "oo" | looks like Y |
| Х х | "kh" (guttural h) | looks like X |

**Uniquely Cyrillic — new sounds**:
| Cyrillic | Sound | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Б б | "b" | *банк* (bank) |
| Г г | "g" | *город* (gorod) — city |
| Д д | "d" | *да* (da) — yes |
| Ж ж | "zh" (like French j) | *жить* (zhit') — to live |
| З з | "z" | *здесь* (zdes') — here |
| И и | "ee" | *или* (ili) — or |
| Й й | "y" (glide, short) | *мой* (moy) — my |
| Л л | "l" (soft) | *лимон* (limon) — lemon |
| П п | "p" | *папа* (papa) |
| Ф ф | "f" | *факт* (fakt) — fact |
| Э э | "e" (plain, no y-glide) | *это* (eto) — this/that |
| Ю ю | "yu" | *юг* (yug) — south |
| Я я | "ya" | *я* (ya) — I |
| Ц ц | "ts" | *центр* (tsentr) — center |
| Ч ч | "ch" | *час* (chas) — hour |
| Ш ш | "sh" | *школа* (shkola) — school |
| Щ щ | "shch" (soft sh) | *щи* (shchi) — cabbage soup |

**The soft and hard signs**:
- **Ь** (soft sign): no sound itself — softens the preceding consonant, making it palatalized (tongue against hard palate): *мать* (mat') — mother vs *мат* (mat) — profanity. Crucial for meaning.
- **Ъ** (hard sign): rare; creates a brief separation: *объект* (ob"yekt) — object.

## Stress and vowel reduction — the hardest part of reading aloud
Russian stress is **free** (falls on different syllables in different words) and **unmarked** in regular text. You must memorize stress per word.

**Akanye (аканье)**: unstressed **О** sounds like **А**: *молоко* (moloko) = spoken "maLAko" (milk). *хорошо* (khorosho) = "khArASHO" (good/fine). This is why Russian sounds different from how it's spelled — the vowels are constantly shifting in quality:
- Stressed О = "o" (clear).
- Unstressed О (first pre-stress syllable) = "a" (reduced).
- Unstressed О (other positions) = schwa "uh".
- Stressed Е = "ye"; unstressed = "yi" or further reduced.

**Practical tip**: dictionaries mark stress with an accent (молоко́). Always note the stressed syllable when you learn a new word.

## Print vs cursive
You will encounter **print Cyrillic** everywhere (books, screens, signs). Russian **cursive** looks dramatically different — г looks like a swooping s, т looks like m, д like g. Recognize it exists; learn to read it only when needed. Start with print exclusively.

# No Articles, No Present "To Be" <!-- slug: no-articles-no-to-be -->
*(Learn this before anything else — it's a relief AND a trap.)*

## No articles
Russian has no equivalent of *the* or *a/an*. Ever. *Книга* can mean "a book," "the book," or "books" depending on context.
Context, word order, and aspect handle definiteness:
- *Я купил книгу.* (ya kupil knigu) — I bought a book / the book.
- Word order can suggest: new info tends to come later: *В комнате был стол* (v komnate byl stol) — In the room **there was** a table (*стол* = new info, indefinite feel). *Стол был в комнате* (stol byl v komnate) — The table was in the room (*стол* = known, definite feel).

✗ Never insert anything for "the" or "a" — there simply is no equivalent. Learn to trust context.

## No present tense of "to be" (быть)
In the **present tense**, the verb *быть* (byt') — to be — is **omitted entirely**:
- *Я студент.* (ya student) — I [am] a student.
- *Она врач.* (ona vrach) — She [is] a doctor.
- *Москва — столица России.* (Moskva — stolitsa Rossii) — Moscow [is] the capital of Russia.
- *Где книга? — Книга на столе.* (gde kniga — kniga na stole) — Where [is] the book? — The book [is] on the table.

A dash (—) is often written between subject and predicate noun in formal writing: *Москва — большой город* (Moscow is a big city).

**The trap**: in past and future, быть appears and must agree:
- Past: *был* (m), *была* (f), *было* (n), *были* (pl) — *Она была дома.* (she was home.)
- Future: *буду, будешь, будет, будем, будете, будут* — used for imperfective future (see Verb Aspect chapter).

**Also omitted**: present forms of other linking verbs like *являться* (to be/constitute) in formal written Russian — they appear but are rarer.

# Nouns, Gender & the Six Cases <!-- slug: nouns-gender-cases -->

This is the spine of Russian grammar. Cases are not a decoration — they ARE the grammar. Every noun, pronoun, and adjective changes its ending depending on its role in the sentence. You can scramble word order and still be understood because the case endings carry the meaning.

## Gender
Russian nouns have **three** genders: masculine, feminine, neuter. Gender is largely predictable from the ending:
- **Masculine**: consonant ending — *стол* (table), *брат* (brother), *город* (city). Also *-ь* words (need to memorize: *день* m, *путь* m).
- **Feminine**: **-а / -я** ending — *книга* (book), *семья* (family); also *-ь* words (*ночь* f, *жизнь* f — must memorize gender for -ь words).
- **Neuter**: **-о / -е** ending — *окно* (window), *море* (sea), *время* (time — irregular).

## The six cases — what each one DOES
| Case | Question it answers | Core function |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominative** | кто? что? (who? what?) | Subject of the verb |
| **Accusative** | кого? что? (whom? what?) | Direct object; direction with в/на |
| **Genitive** | кого? чего? (of whom? of what?) | Possession, absence, quantity |
| **Dative** | кому? чему? (to whom? to what?) | Indirect object; recipient |
| **Instrumental** | кем? чем? (by whom? with what?) | Means, instrument, "with"; predicate noun with быть |
| **Prepositional** | о ком? о чём? (about whom? about what?) | Only used after prepositions (в, на, о, при) |

## Core singular endings — hard-stem nouns
| | Masculine (стол) | Feminine (книга) | Neuter (окно) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | стол | книга | окно |
| Accusative | стол (inaniм.) / стола (anim.) | книгу | окно |
| Genitive | стола | книги | окна |
| Dative | столу | книге | окну |
| Instrumental | столом | книгой | окном |
| Prepositional | столе | книге | окне |

**Animacy** matters for accusative: animate nouns (people, animals) take genitive-like forms: *Я вижу брата* (I see [my] brother — accusative = genitive form of *брат*). Inanimate stays like nominative: *Я вижу стол.*

## What each case feels like in practice

**Nominative**: the default. Subject of any verb. *Студент читает.* (The/a student reads.)

**Accusative**: direct object (what the action hits); also direction INTO a place with в/на: *Я читаю книгу.* (I'm reading a book — *книгу* = accusative of *книга*.) *Я иду в школу.* (I'm going to school — *школу* = accusative.)

**Genitive**: possession (*книга студента* = the student's book — *студента* = genitive); absence with *нет*: *Нет времени* (there's no time — *времени* = genitive of *время*); after numbers 2–4 and 5+; after *много/мало/сколько*: *много книг* (many books).

**Dative**: giving, sending, saying TO someone: *Я дал книгу брату.* (I gave the book to [my] brother — *брату* = dative.) Age construction: *Мне двадцать лет* (ya mne dvadtsat' let — I am twenty years old, lit. "to me twenty years").

**Instrumental**: doing something WITH or BY means of: *Я пишу ручкой* (I write with a pen — *ручкой* = instrumental). Profession/role with быть: *Она была учителем* (she was a teacher). After *с* (with people): *с другом* (with a friend).

**Prepositional**: ONLY after prepositions — о (about), в (in, location), на (on, location), при (under, at the time of): *Я думаю о тебе* (ya dumayu o tebe — I'm thinking about you). *Мы живём в Москве* (my zhivyom v Moskve — we live in Moscow).

# Pronouns <!-- slug: pronouns -->

## Personal pronouns and their case forms
| | Nom | Acc | Gen | Dat | Inst | Prep |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | я | меня | меня | мне | мной | мне |
| you (sg) | ты | тебя | тебя | тебе | тобой | тебе |
| he | он | его* | его | ему | им | нём |
| she | она | её* | её | ей | ей | ней |
| we | мы | нас | нас | нам | нами | нас |
| you (pl/formal) | вы | вас | вас | вам | вами | вас |
| they | они | их* | их | им | ими | них |

*After prepositions, 3rd person pronouns add Н-: *у него, к ней, с ними, о них.* This is mandatory — ✗ ~~у его~~ → ✓ *у него.*

**Вы** (capitalized) is also the formal singular "you" — always use it with strangers, elders, in professional settings. *Ты* is informal (friends, family, children).

## Possessives
Possessives agree in gender, number, and case with the NOUN they modify (not the possessor):
| | m sg | f sg | n sg | pl |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| my | мой | моя | моё | мои |
| your (ты) | твой | твоя | твоё | твои |
| our | наш | наша | наше | наши |
| your (вы) | ваш | ваша | ваше | ваши |

3rd person possessives **don't change** (they don't agree — they're frozen genitive forms): *его* (his), *её* (her), *их* (their): *его книга, его книги, его книге* — *его* stays *его* regardless.

*свой* (svoy) = reflexive possessive "one's own" — refers back to the subject: *Она любит своего мужа* (she loves her [own] husband — *свой* = same subject). Compare: *Она любит его мужа* (she loves his husband — some other man's husband).

# Question Formation <!-- slug: question-formation -->

## Intonation questions (most common)
Simply raise pitch on the key word — no word order change needed:
*Ты говоришь по-русски?* (ty govorish' po-russki?) — Do you speak Russian? (same word order as statement)
*Ты говоришь по-русски?* (emphasis on *ты*) = Is it YOU who speaks Russian?

## Ли (li) — formal/literary yes/no question
Insert **ли** after the verb or the word in question (second position):
*Говоришь ли ты по-русски?* — Do you speak Russian? (more formal)
*Придёт ли он?* — Will he come? — common in reported speech: *Я не знаю, придёт ли он* (I don't know if he will come).

## Question words
| Russian | Transliteration | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| кто | kto | who | *Кто это?* — Who is this? |
| что | chto | what | *Что ты делаешь?* — What are you doing? |
| где | gde | where (location) | *Где ты живёшь?* — Where do you live? |
| куда | kuda | where (direction to) | *Куда ты идёшь?* — Where are you going? |
| откуда | otkuda | where from | *Откуда ты?* — Where are you from? |
| когда | kogda | when | *Когда ты придёшь?* — When will you come? |
| почему | pochemu | why | *Почему ты не пришёл?* — Why didn't you come? |
| зачем | zachem | why / what for (purpose) | *Зачем это?* — What's this for? |
| как | kak | how | *Как дела?* — How are things? |
| сколько | skol'ko | how many/much | *Сколько стоит?* — How much does it cost? |
| какой/-ая/-ое | kakoy | what kind / which | *Какой фильм?* — What film? |
| чей/-ья/-ьё | chey | whose | *Чья это книга?* — Whose book is this? |

- Question words with prepositions — preposition comes first: *С кем ты говорил?* (s kem ty govoril?) — Who did you talk with? *О чём фильм?* — What is the film about?
- *где vs куда*: location (*где ты?* — where are you?) vs direction (*куда ты идёшь?* — where are you going?). This distinction maps onto the locative/directional case difference (prepositional for where, accusative for where-to).

# Verb Aspect — Imperfective vs Perfective <!-- slug: verb-aspect -->
*(The central concept of Russian — as fundamental as ser/estar is for Spanish. You cannot speak Russian without this.)*

Almost every Russian verb comes in a **pair**: an **imperfective** form and a **perfective** form. They describe the same action but from different angles:

| | **Imperfective (несовершенный)** | **Perfective (совершенный)** |
|---|---|---|
| Question | What was happening? Process? | Did it get DONE? Result? |
| Time forms | present, past, future | past and future ONLY (no imperfective present) |
| Feeling | ongoing, repeated, habitual, attempted | completed, one-time, result achieved |

## Common pairs
| Imperfective | Perfective | English gloss |
|---|---|---|
| читать (chitat') | прочитать (prochitat') | to read / to read through |
| писать (pisat') | написать (napisat') | to write / to write (and finish) |
| говорить (govorit') | сказать (skazat') | to speak/talk / to say (once) |
| делать (delat') | сделать (sdelat') | to do / to do (and complete) |
| покупать (pokupat') | купить (kupit') | to buy (process) / to buy (and get it) |
| смотреть (smotret') | посмотреть (posmotret') | to watch / to watch (to the end) |
| учить (uchit') | выучить (vyuchit') | to learn/study / to learn (and know it) |
| давать (davat') | дать (dat') | to give (process) / to give (done) |
| брать (brat') | взять (vzyat') | to take (process) / to take (done) |

Most perfectives are formed by adding a prefix (*на-, про-, с-, вы-, по-, за-*…) or by changing the suffix/stem. Learn them as pairs, not as isolated verbs.

## What each aspect does in different tenses

**Past tense**:
- Imperfective past = was doing, used to do, process: *Я читал книгу.* (ya chital knigu) — I was reading a book / I read [for a while]. Action in progress, or habitual.
- Perfective past = completed, result: *Я прочитал книгу.* (ya prochital knigu) — I [have] read the book [through]. Done.

**The contrast in one exchange**: *Ты читал эту книгу?* (Have you read / Did you read this book — ever, process?) vs *Ты прочитал эту книгу?* (Did you finish reading this book — result?).

**Future tense**:
- Imperfective future = будет + infinitive: *Я буду читать.* (ya budu chitat') — I will be reading / I will read [habitually].
- Perfective future = simple conjugated perfective verb: *Я прочитаю.* (ya prochitayu) — I will read it [and finish].

**Imperatives**: aspect changes the tone:
- Imperfective imperative = ongoing / general instruction: *Читай!* (chital') — Read! [Keep reading.]
- Perfective imperative = single-result request: *Прочитай это!* (prochitay eto) — Read this! [Get through it.]

**Key insight**: perfective verbs have **no present tense**. When you conjugate a perfective in present-looking forms, it IS the future: *прочитаю* = I will have read (future perfective).

## Aspect with negation
Negation almost always pulls toward **imperfective**:
*Я не читал эту книгу.* (I haven't read / didn't read this book — imperfective, open-ended.)
*Я не прочитал эту книгу.* (I didn't finish reading it — perfective, specific failure to complete.)

# Present Tense <!-- slug: present-tense -->

Only **imperfective** verbs have a present tense. There are two conjugation patterns.

## First conjugation (-ать, -ять, -еть endings — mostly)
| person | ending | читать (to read) |
|---|---|---|
| я | **-ю / -у** | читаю (chitayu) |
| ты | **-ешь / -ёшь** | читаешь (chitayesh') |
| он/она | **-ет / -ёт** | читает (chitayet) |
| мы | **-ем / -ём** | читаем (chitayem) |
| вы | **-ете / -ёте** | читаете (chitayete) |
| они | **-ют / -ут** | читают (chitayut) |

## Second conjugation (-ить, -еть, -ать with exceptions)
| person | ending | говорить (to speak) |
|---|---|---|
| я | **-ю / -у** | говорю (govoryu) |
| ты | **-ишь** | говоришь (govorish') |
| он/она | **-ит** | говорит (govorit) |
| мы | **-им** | говорим (govorim) |
| вы | **-ите** | говорите (govorite) |
| они | **-ят / -ат** | говорят (govoryat) |

## Spelling mutations in 1st person singular (2nd conjugation)
Common consonant changes: *т → ч* (хотеть → хочу), *д → ж* (ходить → хожу), *з → ж* (возить → вожу), *с → ш* (писать → пишу), *в → вл* (любить → люблю), *б → бл* (ответить → отвечу — actually *т→ч*). These only affect the **я** form; all others are regular.

## Key irregular verbs
| verb | я | ты | он | мы | вы | они |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| быть (to be — future!) | буду | будешь | будет | будем | будете | будут |
| идти (to go on foot) | иду | идёшь | идёт | идём | идёте | идут |
| хотеть (to want) | хочу | хочешь | хочет | хотим | хотите | хотят |
| давать (to give) | даю | даёшь | даёт | даём | даёте | дают |
| знать (to know) | знаю | знаешь | знает | знаем | знаете | знают |

# Past Tense <!-- slug: past-tense -->

Russian past tense is beautifully simple in endings — but aspect (see above) determines meaning.

## Formation: infinitive stem + gender/number suffix
Remove **-ть** from infinitive, add:
| | ending | example (читать) |
|---|---|---|
| masculine (он) | **-л** | читал (chital) |
| feminine (она) | **-ла** | читала (chitala) |
| neuter (оно) | **-ло** | читало (chitalo) |
| plural (они) | **-ли** | читали (chitali) |

The past tense agrees with the **subject's gender** — not the verb's aspect:
*Он прочитал книгу.* (He read the book — done.) *Она прочитала книгу.* *Они читали.* (They were reading.)

## Irregular past stems
- *идти → шёл / шла / шло / шли* (went on foot — completely different stem).
- *мочь → мог / могла / могло / могли* (could).
- *нести → нёс / несла* (carried).
- Verbs ending in *-сти, -зти* often drop the т: *вести → вёл, везти → вёз, класть → клал*.

## What the past tenses MEAN (aspect recap in context)
- *Я читал весь вечер.* (I read / was reading all evening — imperfective, duration.) 
- *Я прочитал книгу за два часа.* (I read the book in two hours — perfective, completed.)
- *Она писала письмо.* (She was writing a letter — process, may not be done.)
- *Она написала письмо.* (She wrote/has written the letter — it's done.)

# Future Tense <!-- slug: future-tense -->

Two totally different structures depending on aspect — this is where aspect shows up most clearly:

## Imperfective future — ongoing/repeated future action
**буду + imperfective infinitive** (буду = будешь = будет = будем = будете = будут):
*Я буду читать завтра.* (ya budu chitat' zavtra) — I will be reading tomorrow / I will read [habitually].
*Мы будем работать.* (We will be working / will work.)

## Perfective future — completed future action
Conjugate the **perfective verb** in present-tense-looking forms — they ARE the future:
*Я прочитаю книгу.* (I will read the book [through].) *Она напишет письмо.* (She will write the letter [and finish it].) *Мы купим билеты.* (We will buy the tickets.)

**The critical difference**:
- *Я буду смотреть фильм.* (I'll be watching a film — process, maybe not to the end.)
- *Я посмотрю фильм.* (I'll watch the film — I'll watch it through, complete.)

# Verbs of Motion <!-- slug: verbs-of-motion -->
*(Language-specific chapter: Russian has a dedicated system for motion that is deeply embedded in everyday speech — no other major Western language does this quite the same way.)*

Russian distinguishes not just "go" but *how you go* and *whether it's a specific trip or general capability.* Then prefixes restructure everything.

## The basic pairs: unidirectional vs multidirectional

| Unidirectional (one trip, right now) | Multidirectional (repeated, round-trip, general) | English |
|---|---|---|
| **идти** (idti) | **ходить** (khodit') | to go on foot |
| **ехать** (yekhat') | **ездить** (yezdit') | to go by vehicle |
| **лететь** (letet') | **летать** (letat') | to fly |
| **плыть** (plyt') | **плавать** (plavat') | to swim/sail |
| **бежать** (bezhat') | **бегать** (begat') | to run |
| **нести** (nesti) | **носить** (nosit') | to carry (on foot) |
| **везти** (vezti) | **возить** (vozit') | to carry (by vehicle) |

**The logic**:
- *Unidirectional* = right now going in one direction, one specific trip: *Я иду в магазин.* (ya idu v magazin — I'm going to the store [right now, this trip].)
- *Multidirectional* = general ability, habitual, round-trip: *Я хожу в магазин каждый день.* (ya khozhu v magazin kazhdiy den' — I go to the store every day.) *Он умеет ходить.* (He can walk.) *Мы ходили в театр.* (We went to the theater — implying we went and came back, a round trip in the past.)

## Prefixes transform everything
Add a prefix to a verb of motion and you get a new verb with a new meaning — and now the unidirectional/multidirectional distinction collapses into imperfective/perfective:

| Prefix | Added to ходить/идти | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| при- | прийти / приходить | to arrive (on foot) |
| у- | уйти / уходить | to leave, go away |
| вы- | выйти / выходить | to go out, exit |
| вой- / в- | войти / входить | to go in, enter |
| за- | зайти / заходить | to stop by, drop in |
| пере- | перейти / переходить | to cross (a street, etc.) |
| под- | подойти / подходить | to approach, come up to |
| от- | отойти / отходить | to step away, move away |
| про- | пройти / проходить | to pass (by/through) |
| до- | дойти / доходить | to reach, get to (on foot) |

*прийти* is perfective (arrive, one-time result); *приходить* is imperfective (arrive repeatedly/generally).

**Examples**:
- *Он пришёл.* (on prishyol) — He arrived / He came. (perfective past)
- *Она приходит каждый день.* (She comes/arrives every day — imperfective present, habitual.)
- *Войдите, пожалуйста.* (Voydite, pozhaluysta) — Please come in. (perfective imperative)
- *Перейди улицу!* (Pereydi ulitsu!) — Cross the street! (perfective imperative)

**Same prefix system applies to ехать/ездить, лететь/летать, etc.**: *приехать / приезжать* (to arrive by vehicle); *улететь / улетать* (to fly away).

# Adjectives & Adverbs <!-- slug: adjectives-adverbs -->

## Adjective agreement
Russian adjectives agree with their noun in **gender, number, AND case** — six cases × three genders × singular/plural = many forms. The pattern is regular once you know the endings.

## Hard-stem adjective endings (singular)
| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | нов**ый** | нов**ая** | нов**ое** |
| Accusative (inanim.) | нов**ый** | нов**ую** | нов**ое** |
| Accusative (anim.) | нов**ого** | нов**ую** | нов**ое** |
| Genitive | нов**ого** | нов**ой** | нов**ого** |
| Dative | нов**ому** | нов**ой** | нов**ому** |
| Instrumental | нов**ым** | нов**ой** | нов**ым** |
| Prepositional | нов**ом** | нов**ой** | нов**ом** |

Plural nominative: *новые*; plural genitive: *новых*; plural dative: *новым* — plural forms don't distinguish gender.

**Soft-stem adjectives** (those ending in -ний like *синий* = blue, *последний* = last): swap hard vowels for soft (*-ый → -ий, -ого → -его*, etc.). The structure is the same.

**Spelling rule**: after г, к, х, ж, ш, щ, ч — write **и** not **ы**: *маленький* (small, m), *русский* (Russian). After ж, ш, щ, ч — write **о** only when stressed, **е** when unstressed.

## Short-form adjectives
Some adjectives have a short predicative form used after *быть*: *нов* (m), *нова* (f), *ново* (n), *новы* (pl). Common in fixed phrases: *рад / рада* (glad), *готов / готова* (ready), *прав / права* (right/correct), *должен / должна* (must/supposed to — extremely common).
*Я рад тебя видеть.* (I'm glad to see you.) *Ты должна позвонить.* (You [f] must/should call.)

## Comparison
- Comparative: **более + adj** or short comparative form (*новее, быстрее, красивее*): *Она умнее меня.* (She's smarter than me — *меня* = genitive after comparison.)
- Common irregular comparatives: *хороший → лучше* (better), *плохой → хуже* (worse), *большой → больше* (bigger, more), *маленький → меньше* (smaller, less), *старый → старше* (older), *молодой → моложе* (younger).
- Superlative: *самый + adjective* (самый умный = the smartest): *Это самый интересный фильм.* *Самый* agrees in full like a regular adjective.

## Adverbs
Most adverbs are simply the neuter short form of the adjective: *быстрый → быстро* (quickly), *красивый → красиво* (beautifully), *плохой → плохо* (badly), *хороший → хорошо* (well/good).
*Он говорит быстро.* (He speaks quickly.)
Key: *очень* (very), *уже* (already), *ещё* (still/yet/more), *тоже* (also), *тут/здесь* (here), *там* (there), *всегда* (always), *никогда* (never), *часто* (often), *редко* (rarely).

# Prepositions and the Cases They Govern <!-- slug: prepositions -->

Prepositions in Russian are inseparable from case — you CANNOT use a preposition without knowing which case it takes.

## в and на — the most essential pair
Both mean roughly "in/on" but they behave differently:

**в (v)** = enclosed space (in, into):
- + **prepositional** (location): *в Москве* (in Moscow), *в школе* (in school), *в комнате* (in the room).
- + **accusative** (direction/destination): *в Москву* (to Moscow — direction), *в школу* (to school), *в комнату* (into the room).

**на (na)** = surface or open space (on, at):
- + **prepositional** (location): *на столе* (on the table), *на улице* (on the street/outside), *на работе* (at work), *на концерте* (at a concert).
- + **accusative** (direction): *на стол* (onto the table), *на улицу* (out to the street), *на работу* (to work), *на концерт* (to a concert).

**Which to use — в or на?** For most enclosed places: в (*в магазине, в библиотеке*). For open areas, events, transport infrastructure: на (*на рынке, на вокзале, на станции, на площади*). Some are idiomatic — memorize: *на работе/на работу* (at/to work), *на улице/на улицу* (outside), *на севере/юге* (in the north/south).

## Key prepositions and their cases
| Preposition | Case | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **у** | genitive | at/by (someone's place), possession | *У меня есть книга.* (I have a book.) |
| **из** | genitive | out of, from (inside) | *Я из России.* (I'm from Russia.) |
| **от** | genitive | away from, from (person) | *Письмо от мамы.* (A letter from mom.) |
| **до** | genitive | until, up to | *До свидания. До Москвы 500 км.* |
| **без** | genitive | without | *Кофе без сахара.* (Coffee without sugar.) |
| **для** | genitive | for (purpose/intended for) | *Подарок для тебя.* (A gift for you.) |
| **после** | genitive | after | *После урока.* (After the lesson.) |
| **к** | dative | toward (person), by (deadline) | *Иди к врачу.* (Go to the doctor.) |
| **по** | dative | along, by, according to | *По улице. По-русски. По телефону.* |
| **с / со** | instrumental | with (together), from surface | *С другом. Со стола.* |
| **над** | instrumental | above | *Над городом.* (Above the city.) |
| **под** | instrumental | under | *Под столом.* (Under the table.) |
| **между** | instrumental | between | *Между нами.* (Between us.) |
| **перед** | instrumental | in front of, before | *Перед домом.* (In front of the house.) |
| **о / об** | prepositional | about | *О чём думаешь?* (What are you thinking about?) |
| **при** | prepositional | at the time of, in the presence of | *При мне. При Советском Союзе.* |

## "I have" — у меня есть
Russian expresses possession with *у + genitive + есть + nominative*: *У меня есть машина.* (ya mne est' mashina — I have a car, lit. "by me there is a car"). Absence: *У меня нет машины.* (no car — *нет* + genitive).

# Numbers and Case Agreement <!-- slug: numbers-cases -->

Numbers in Russian interact with cases in a notoriously irregular pattern. Learn this early — you'll need it for prices, age, quantities.

## The pattern

| Number | Case of noun | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (один) | **Nominative singular** | *один стол, одна книга* |
| 2, 3, 4 (два, три, четыре) | **Genitive singular** | *два стола, три книги, четыре окна* |
| 5–20 and beyond (пять, шесть…) | **Genitive plural** | *пять столов, десять книг, двадцать окон* |
| 21, 31, 41… (ends in 1) | **Nominative singular** (by the 1) | *двадцать один стол* |
| 22, 23, 24, 32, 33… (ends in 2, 3, 4) | **Genitive singular** | *двадцать два стола* |

This is NOT a corner case — it affects everything: *три часа* (3 hours — genitive singular), *пять часов* (5 hours — genitive plural). Getting this wrong is the most audible foreigner mistake in Russian.

## Numbers 1–10 (nominative)
*один (m) / одна (f) / одно (n), два (m/n) / две (f), три, четыре, пять, шесть, семь, восемь, девять, десять.*

*Два* vs *две*: два with masculine and neuter (*два стола, два окна*), две with feminine (*две книги*).

## Age with дать/лет
*Сколько тебе лет?* (skol'ko tebe let?) — How old are you? (lit. "how many years to you?")
*Мне двадцать один год.* (21 — год = nominative sg because of 1)
*Мне двадцать два года.* (22 — года = genitive sg because of 2)
*Мне двадцать пять лет.* (25 — лет = genitive plural because of 5)

# Negation <!-- slug: negation -->

## Basic negation — не (ne)
Place **не** directly before the word being negated (usually the verb):
*Я не знаю.* (ya ne znayu) — I don't know.
*Она не придёт.* (She won't come.)
*Это не правда.* (This is not true.)

## Double negation — required in Russian
Unlike English (one negative per clause), Russian requires **every negative word to also have не**:
*Я ничего не знаю.* (ya nichego ne znayu) — I know nothing / I don't know anything. (Both *ничего* AND *не* present.)
*Никто не пришёл.* (nikto ne prishyol) — Nobody came. (Both *никто* AND *не*.)
*Я никогда не курил.* (ya nikogda ne kuril) — I've never smoked.
*Нигде нет мест.* (nigde net mest) — There are no seats anywhere.

Double (or triple) negation is grammatically **correct** and required: ✓ *Я ни с кем никогда ни о чём не говорю.* (I never talk to anyone about anything.)

## Genitive of negation
After **нет** (there is no) and **не** with verbs of being/having, the noun shifts to **genitive**:
*Здесь нет воды.* (Here there is no water — *воды* = genitive of *вода*.)
*Я не вижу машины.* (I don't see a/the car — *машины* = genitive, slightly more emphatic negation than accusative *машину*.)
*У меня нет времени.* (I have no time — *времени* = genitive of *время*.)

## Negative words
*ничто / ничего* (nothing), *никто / никого* (nobody), *никогда* (never), *нигде* (nowhere — location), *никуда* (nowhere — direction), *никак* (no way/somehow not), *ни … ни …* (neither … nor): *Ни мясо, ни рыбу.* (Neither meat nor fish.)

# Conjunctions & Complex Sentences <!-- slug: conjunctions-complex-sentences -->

## Key conjunctions
| Russian | Translit | Meaning | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **и** | i | and | *Я и ты.* |
| **а** | a | and/but (contrast) | *Я иду, а ты остаёшься.* (I'm going, and you're staying — contrast) |
| **но** | no | but (opposition) | *Хочу, но не могу.* (I want to but can't.) |
| **или** | ili | or | *Чай или кофе?* |
| **что** | chto | that | *Я знаю, что ты прав.* |
| **потому что** | potomu chto | because | *Я устал, потому что работал.* |
| **чтобы** | chtoby | in order to / so that | *Учись, чтобы знать.* + subjunctive mood |
| **если** | yesli | if | *Если хочешь, скажи.* |
| **когда** | kogda | when | *Когда придёшь, позвони.* |
| **хотя** | khotya | although | *Хотя устал, продолжил.* |
| **пока** | poka | while / until | *Пока ты спал, я работал.* |
| **как только** | kak tol'ko | as soon as | *Как только приду, позвоню.* |

## что vs чтобы
- **что** (that) + **indicative** — reports facts: *Я думаю, что ты прав.* (I think that you're right.)
- **чтобы** (so that / in order to) + **past subjunctive** — purpose or wish: *Я хочу, чтобы ты пришёл.* (ya khochu, chtoby ty prishyol) — I want you to come (lit. "I want so that you came"). *Он изучает русский, чтобы читать Толстого.* (He studies Russian in order to read Tolstoy — here *чтобы* + infinitive, same subject.)

## Который — the relative pronoun that declines
*который / которая / которое / которые* = who / which / that (relative pronoun). Unlike English, it must agree in **gender and number** with its antecedent AND take the **case** required by its role in the relative clause:
*Книга, **которую** я читаю…* (The book that I'm reading — *которую* = accusative f because it's the object of *читаю*.)
*Человек, **которому** я дал книгу…* (The person to whom I gave the book — *которому* = dative m because of *дал*.)
*Студенты, **с которыми** я учусь…* (The students with whom I study — *с которыми* = instrumental plural because of *с*.)

# Conditionals — бы <!-- slug: conditionals -->

Russian conditionals use **бы** — an invariable particle — to mark irreality/subjunctive.

## Real conditions — если + indicative
*Если ты придёшь, я буду рад.* (yesli ty pridyosh', ya budu rad) — If you come, I'll be glad. (Future perfective in if-clause; future imperfective in result.)

## Unreal conditions — если бы + past, ... бы + past
For hypothetical (unreal) conditions, Russian uses **если бы** + past tense (imperfective for ongoing, perfective for single events), and **бы** + past in the result clause:
*Если бы я знал, я бы сказал.* (yesli by ya znal, ya by skazal) — If I had known / If I knew, I would have said / I would say.
*Если бы у меня было время, я бы помог.* (If I had time, I would help.)

**Бы** can go almost anywhere in the result clause — it floats, but typically follows the subject or the verb: *Я бы пошёл. Пошёл бы я. Я пошёл бы.* All fine.

**No tense distinction for present vs past unreal** — context clarifies: *Если бы я был богатым* can mean "if I were rich [now]" or "if I had been rich [then]."

# Imperatives <!-- slug: imperatives -->

## Formation
From the 3rd person plural (они form), remove ending, add:
- If stem ends in a **vowel**: add **й**: *читают → читай* (chita**y**) — read!
- If stem ends in a **consonant + stress on ending**: add **и**: *говорят → говори* — speak!
- If stem ends in a **consonant + stress NOT on ending**: add **ь**: *режут → режь* — cut!

Plural/formal (вы): add **-те**: *читайте, говорите, режьте.*

## Aspect in imperatives
- Imperfective = general/ongoing/repeated instruction: *Читайте каждый день.* (Read every day.) *Говорите медленнее.* (Speak slower — ongoing.)
- Perfective = single-action request, often more urgent: *Прочитайте это.* (Read this — [and] get through it.) *Скажите мне.* (Tell me — once.)
- Perfective negative = warning/prohibition (don't accidentally do): *Не потеряй ключи!* (Don't lose the keys — perfective: don't let it happen.) Imperfective negative = stop / don't habitually do: *Не теряй время.* (Don't waste time — imperfective: general habit.)

## Common imperatives to know immediately
*Скажи/скажите* (tell me/say), *дай/дайте* (give me), *иди/идите* (go — on foot), *войдите* (come in), *подожди/подождите* (wait), *смотри/смотрите* (look), *слушай/слушайте* (listen), *помоги/помогите* (help), *позвони* (call me), *извини/извините* (excuse me/sorry), *объясни* (explain).

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

Plot in **present** — same convention as in English.

**Facts**:
*Это комедия / триллер / драма / боевик (action) / мелодрама / документальный фильм.*
*Фильм снят режиссёром…* (snyal rezhissyorom — the film was shot by the director…)
*В главной роли — …* (v glavnoy roli — in the main role is…)
*Действие происходит в Москве в 1990-х годах.* (deystviye proiskhodit — the action takes place in Moscow in the 1990s.)
*Фильм основан на книге / на реальных событиях.* (based on a book / real events)

**Plot**:
*Фильм о / про …* (Fil'm o / pro — The film is about…) — *про* is more colloquial.
*Главный герой хочет…, но…* (Glavny geroy khochet — the main hero wants… but…)
*В конце выясняется, что…* (V kontse vyyasnyayetsya, chto — At the end it turns out that…)

**Opinion**:
*Мне очень понравился фильм.* (Mne ochen' ponravilsya fil'm — I really liked the film — *понравился* agrees with *фильм*, m.)
*Мне не понравилось.* (I didn't like it.) *Фильм меня тронул.* (The film moved me.) *Скучновато.* (A bit boring.) *Очень захватывающий.* (very gripping.) *Рекомендую!* (I recommend it!) *Стоит посмотреть.* (Worth watching.)

**Vocabulary**:
- People: *актёр / актриса, режиссёр, главный герой / героиня, злодей (villain), персонаж (character).*
- Story: *сюжет (plot), концовка / финал (ending), сцена (scene), саундтрек, спецэффекты.*
- Adjectives: *захватывающий (gripping), трогательный (moving/touching), смешной (funny), страшный (scary), скучный (boring), предсказуемый (predictable), неожиданный (unexpected/surprising), глубокий (deep/profound).*
- ✓ *Только что посмотрел фильм — это триллер, действие которого происходит в 80-х. Главный герой пытается выяснить, кто убил его брата. Мне очень понравился сюжет и концовка была неожиданной.*
