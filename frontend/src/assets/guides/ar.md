> **Note on variety**: This guide teaches **Modern Standard Arabic (MSA / الفصحى, al-fuṣḥā)** — the formal written standard used in news, literature, education, and formal speech across the Arab world. Spoken dialects (Egyptian, Levantine, Gulf, Maghrebi, etc.) differ substantially in vocabulary, pronunciation, and grammar. MSA is your entry point into all of them and into reading.

---

# The Arabic Script & Pronunciation <!-- slug: script-pronunciation -->

Arabic is written **right to left** in a connected, cursive script. There is no capital/lowercase distinction. The alphabet has **28 letters**, each with up to **four forms** depending on where it falls in a word (isolated, initial, medial, final) — but the core shape stays recognizable.

## Letter position forms <!-- slug: letter-forms -->

| Letter | Isolated | Initial | Medial | Final | Transliteration |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ب | ب | بـ | ـبـ | ـب | b |
| ع | ع | عـ | ـعـ | ـع | ʿ |
| م | م | مـ | ـمـ | ـم | m |

**Six letters** (ا و ر ز د ذ) only connect to the preceding letter — they break the cursive chain: *كَتَبَ* joins fluidly, but *وَرَقَة* (paper) has a break after the و.

## Sun letters and moon letters <!-- slug: sun-moon-letters -->

The definite article is **الـ (al-)** — but the *l* assimilates to 14 of the 28 consonants:

**Sun letters** (الحروف الشمسية) — the *l* assimilates: ت ث د ذ ر ز س ش ص ض ط ظ ن ل
- الشَّمْس (*al-shams* → *ash-shams*) — the sun
- النَّهْر (*al-nahr* → *an-nahr*) — the river

**Moon letters** (الحروف القمرية) — *l* stays: ا ب ج ح خ ع غ ف ق ك م و هـ ي
- القَمَر (*al-qamar*) — the moon (stays *al-*)
- الكِتَاب (*al-kitāb*) — the book

Mnemonic: **moon** letter = *al-* stays, just like *al-qamar* itself keeps the *al-*.

## Emphatic consonants <!-- slug: emphatic-consonants -->

Arabic has a group of **pharyngealized / "emphatic" consonants** that darken the vowels around them, making the entire syllable sound "heavier":

| Letter | Transliteration | Plain equivalent | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ص | ṣ | s | صَبَر (ṣabar) — patience |
| ض | ḍ | d | ضَرَبَ (ḍaraba) — he hit |
| ط | ṭ | t | طَالِب (ṭālib) — student |
| ظ | ẓ | z/dh | ظَهَر (ẓahara) — appeared |

The *ض* (ḍ) is considered the most distinctive Arabic sound — Arabic is sometimes called "the language of ḍ" (لغة الضاد). English speakers: tense your tongue and lower your jaw for these — they're not just soft d/t/s/z.

## Difficult sounds for English speakers <!-- slug: difficult-sounds -->

| Sound | Letter | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ع | ʿayn (ع) | A voiced pharyngeal fricative — a constriction deep in the throat, voiced. Like gagging slightly on a vowel. No English equivalent. | عَرَبِيّ (ʿarabī) — Arabic |
| ح | ḥ (ح) | A strong breathy *h* from the throat — much more friction than English h | حَال (ḥāl) — condition |
| خ | kh (خ) | Like German *ch* in "Bach" or Scottish "loch" — velar fricative | خَبَر (khabar) — news |
| غ | gh (غ) | Voiced version of خ — like a French *r* (gargling) | غَنِيّ (ghanī) — rich |
| ق | q (ق) | A *k* made at the very back of the throat (uvular) | قَلْب (qalb) — heart |
| ء | hamza (ء) | A glottal stop — the pause in "uh-oh". Written in several ways. | أَهْل (ahl) — family |

## Short vowels, long vowels, and harakat <!-- slug: vowels-harakat -->

Arabic has **three short vowels** and **three long vowels**:

| Short | Long | Sound |
|---|---|---|
| َ fatha (a) | ا ā | "ah" — short vs long |
| ِ kasra (i) | ي ī | "ee" — short vs long |
| ُ ḍamma (u) | و ū | "oo" — short vs long |

**Harakat (حَرَكَات)** are the small diacritical marks (dots and dashes above/below letters) that write these short vowels. In formal Arabic (Quran, children's books, language-learning texts), they are written. In newspapers, novels, and everyday text, **they are omitted** — readers infer them from context and their knowledge of the root-and-pattern system. This is why vocabulary comes from patterns, not spelling alone.

**Sukūn (ْ)** marks a consonant with no following vowel. **Shadda (ّ)** doubles a consonant.

**Tanwīn (nunation)**: adding *n* sound to an indefinite noun's case ending — see the Case Endings chapter.

# The Root & Pattern System <!-- slug: root-pattern-system -->

*(Language-specific chapter: the master key to Arabic vocabulary — learn this and the language unlocks.)*

Arabic vocabulary is built on **triliteral roots** — most words derive from a **3-consonant root** (sometimes 4-consonant). You **extract the pattern** by applying vowels and affixes to those three root consonants, producing a family of related words.

## The k-t-b root (ك ت ب) — writing <!-- slug: root-ktb-example -->

| Word | Arabic | Meaning | Pattern applied |
|---|---|---|---|
| kataba | كَتَبَ | he wrote | faʿala (past verb) |
| yaktubu | يَكْتُبُ | he writes | yafʿulu (present verb) |
| kitāb | كِتَاب | book | fiʿāl (noun of thing) |
| kātib | كَاتِب | writer/scribe | fāʿil (active participle) |
| maktūb | مَكْتُوب | written / letter | mafʿūl (passive participle) |
| maktab | مَكْتَب | office / desk | mafʿal (place noun) |
| kitāba | كِتَابَة | writing (the act) | fiʿāla (verbal noun of action) |
| kuttāb | كُتَّاب | writers (plural) | fuʿʿāl (intensive plural) |

The root ك-ت-ب runs through all of them. Once you recognize it, you understand that *maktab* means "place of writing" = office, without being told.

## Another root: d-r-s (د ر س) — studying <!-- slug: root-drs-example -->

- دَرَسَ (*darasa*) — he studied
- يَدْرُسُ (*yadrusu*) — he studies
- دِرَاسَة (*dirāsa*) — study / studies (the field)
- دَرْس (*dars*) — lesson
- مَدْرَسَة (*madrasa*) — school (place of study)
- مُدَرِّس (*mudarris*) — teacher (intensive form — Form II verb pattern)

**Madrasa** — you've heard this word. Now you know it literally means "the place where studying happens."

## Why this matters for learning <!-- slug: root-pattern-learning -->

- When you learn one word from a root, related words become guessable
- Dictionaries are organized by root — knowing the root lets you navigate
- Broken plurals (see below) are also root-based — less memorization than it looks
- The pattern فَاعِل (*fāʿil*) ALWAYS means "the one who does" — so any *fāʿil*-shaped word is an agent noun

**Practice**: when you see an unknown word, strip out the vowels and identify the three consonants. Then ask: what root is this? What patterns do I know for this root?

# Nouns, Gender & the Definite Article الـ <!-- slug: nouns-gender-article -->

## Gender <!-- slug: gender -->

Arabic nouns are either **masculine** or **feminine** — grammatical gender, not biological.

- Most feminine nouns end in **ة (tāʾ marbūṭa)** — a special *t* that sounds like *a/ah* in pause, *at* when followed by a vowel:
  - مَدْرَسَة (*madrasa*) — school; كِتَابَة (*kitāba*) — writing; طَالِبَة (*ṭāliba*) — female student

- Feminine by nature without the ending: body parts in pairs (يَد *yad* — hand, عَيْن *ʿayn* — eye), some countries and cities, certain broken plurals

- Masculine: everything else by default

## The definite article الـ (al-) <!-- slug: definite-article -->

- Prefix **الـ** to any noun to make it definite: كِتَاب (*kitāb*, a book) → اَلْكِتَاب (*al-kitāb*, the book)
- The *a* in *al-* is dropped after a vowel-final word in connected speech (called *waṣl*)
- Sun-letter assimilation applies (see Script chapter)
- Arabic uses the definite article in some places English doesn't: with abstract nouns and generics:
  - اَلْعِلْم (*al-ʿilm*) — knowledge / Science in general
  - اَلْحَيَاة (*al-ḥayāt*) — life (in general)

**Indefinite = no article**, often with tanwīn (nunation): كِتَابٌ (*kitābun*, a book).

# Case Endings & Tanwīn (Nunation) <!-- slug: case-endings-tanwin -->

Classical and formal MSA has **three cases** marked by short vowel endings:

| Case | Ending | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Nominative | -u (ضَمَّة) | subject |
| Accusative | -a (فَتْحَة) | direct object, adverbs |
| Genitive | -i (كَسْرَة) | after prepositions, in iḍāfa |

**Indefinite nouns** add *-n* (tanwīn/nunation) to these endings:
- كِتَابٌ (*kitābun*) — a book (nominative indefinite)
- كِتَابًا (*kitāban*) — a book (accusative indefinite)
- كِتَابٍ (*kitābin*) — a book (genitive indefinite)

**In practice**: case endings are written in fully voweled texts and are audible in formal recitation and news broadcasting. In everyday conversation and most reading, they are dropped. Focus on recognizing them, not on producing them perfectly from day one — but knowing they exist explains why words end differently in different contexts.

# Number — Singular, Dual, Plural <!-- slug: number-dual-plural -->

Arabic has **three numbers**: singular, **dual** (exactly two), and plural.

## Dual — the distinctive two <!-- slug: dual -->

Add **-ān** (nominative) or **-ayn** (accusative/genitive) to the singular:
- كِتَاب (*kitāb*) → كِتَابَان (*kitābān*) — two books (nominative) / كِتَابَيْن (*kitābayn*) — two books (acc/gen)
- طَالِب (*ṭālib*) → طَالِبَان (*ṭālibān*) — two students
- يَوْم (*yawm*) → يَوْمَان (*yawmān*) — two days

Dual is mandatory and precise — you cannot use "two" + singular the way Chinese uses measure words.

## Sound Plurals <!-- slug: sound-plurals -->

Regular plurals that preserve the singular form:

**Masculine sound plural**: add -ūn (nominative) or -īn (accusative/genitive)
- مُدَرِّس → مُدَرِّسُون / مُدَرِّسِين (*mudarrisūna/mudarrisīna*) — teachers (male)

**Feminine sound plural**: replace ة with ات (-āt)
- طَالِبَة → طَالِبَات (*ṭālibāt*) — female students
- سَيَّارَة → سَيَّارَات (*sayyārāt*) — cars

## Broken Plurals — the surprise <!-- slug: broken-plurals -->

Most Arabic nouns form their plural by **internally restructuring** the root — the "template" changes. These must be learned per word, but they follow recognizable patterns:

| Singular | Plural | Pattern change |
|---|---|---|
| كِتَاب (*kitāb*) | كُتُب (*kutub*) | books |
| بَيْت (*bayt*) | بُيُوت (*buyūt*) | houses |
| رَجُل (*rajul*) | رِجَال (*rijāl*) | men |
| كَلِمَة (*kalima*) | كَلِمَات (*kalimāt*) | words (takes -āt!) |
| وَلَد (*walad*) | أَوْلَاد (*awlād*) | boys/children |
| مَدِينَة (*madīna*) | مُدُن (*mudun*) | cities |
| عَيْن (*ʿayn*) | عُيُون (*ʿuyūn*) | eyes |

**The famous grammatical surprise**: **non-human plurals take feminine singular agreement**. If the noun is a thing (not a person), its plural verb and adjective take the **feminine singular** form:

- الكُتُب **مَوْجُودَة** — The books are available. (مَوْجُودَة = feminine singular, not plural)
- السَّيَّارَات **سَرِيعَة** — Cars are fast. (سَرِيعَة = feminine singular)

This applies to animals and objects. Only human plurals get plural agreement.

# Pronouns — Independent and Suffix <!-- slug: pronouns -->

## Independent pronouns <!-- slug: independent-pronouns -->

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | أَنَا anā (I) | نَحْنُ naḥnu (we) |
| 2nd m. | أَنْتَ anta (you, m) | أَنْتُمْ antum (you all, m) |
| 2nd f. | أَنْتِ anti (you, f) | أَنْتُنَّ antunna (you all, f) |
| 3rd m. | هُوَ huwa (he/it) | هُمْ hum (they, m) |
| 3rd f. | هِيَ hiya (she/it) | هُنَّ hunna (they, f) |

MSA strictly distinguishes masculine and feminine in 2nd and 3rd person. Dual forms (هُمَا *humā* — those two) also exist.

## Suffix (attached) pronouns <!-- slug: suffix-pronouns -->

These attach to nouns (possession), verbs (objects), and prepositions — they are high-frequency and must be memorized:

| Person | Suffix | Example on كِتَاب |
|---|---|---|
| my | ـي (-ī) | كِتَابِي (*kitābī*) — my book |
| your (m) | ـكَ (-ka) | كِتَابُكَ (*kitābuka*) — your book |
| your (f) | ـكِ (-ki) | كِتَابُكِ (*kitābuki*) — your book |
| his | ـهُ (-hu) | كِتَابُهُ (*kitābuhu*) — his book |
| her | ـهَا (-hā) | كِتَابُهَا (*kitābuhā*) — her book |
| our | ـنَا (-nā) | كِتَابُنَا (*kitābunā*) — our book |
| their (m) | ـهُمْ (-hum) | كِتَابُهُمْ (*kitābuhum*) — their book |

The same suffixes attach to verbs (*ضَرَبَهُ* — he hit him) and prepositions (*مَعَهُ* — with him).

# The Nominal Sentence — No Present "To Be" <!-- slug: nominal-sentence -->

*(Language-specific: one of the first structural surprises.)*

In Arabic, **there is no present-tense verb "to be."** A noun or pronoun subject + a predicate (noun, adjective, or prepositional phrase) forms a complete sentence:

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|---|---|---|
| مُحَمَّدٌ طَالِبٌ | Muḥammadun ṭālibun | Muhammad is a student |
| البَيْتُ كَبِيرٌ | al-baytu kabīrun | The house is big |
| هِيَ مِصْرِيَّةٌ | hiya miṣriyyatun | She is Egyptian |
| الكِتَابُ عَلَى الطَّاوِلَةِ | al-kitābu ʿalā al-ṭāwila | The book is on the table |

The subject is **definite** (with article or proper noun) and the predicate is usually **indefinite** (no article). This subject-definite / predicate-indefinite rule is how you tell them apart.

For past tense: **كَانَ** (*kāna* — he/it was) is used, and it conjugates. For future: **سَيَكُونُ** (*sayakūnu*) or **سَوْفَ يَكُونُ**.

# Adjectives <!-- slug: adjectives -->

Adjectives **follow the noun** they modify and must agree in **gender, number, and definiteness**:

- بَيْتٌ كَبِيرٌ (*baytun kabīrun*) — a big house (m. sg. indefinite)
- البَيْتُ الكَبِيرُ (*al-baytu al-kabīru*) — the big house (both definite)
- طَالِبَةٌ ذَكِيَّةٌ (*ṭālibatun dhakiyyatun*) — a clever female student
- الطَّالِبَاتُ الذَّكِيَّاتُ (*al-ṭālibātu al-dhakiyyātu*) — the clever female students

**Definiteness agreement is a common trap**: if the noun has the article, the adjective must too. Lacking the article on the adjective makes it a predicate ("the student is clever"), not an attributive ("the clever student").

Human plural agreement: adjective takes sound plural.
Non-human plural: adjective takes **feminine singular** (the same rule as verbs — see Broken Plurals).

# The Iḍāfa — Noun Construct Chain <!-- slug: idafa-construct -->

*(Language-specific chapter: the primary possessive and compound noun structure.)*

**Iḍāfa (الإضافة)** means "addition." It's a chain of two or more nouns where the first (the "possessed" or modified noun) is in **construct state** — no article, no tanwīn — and the second (the "possessor" or specifier) is in the **genitive case** and may be definite or indefinite.

**A + B (genitive) = "A of B" or "B's A"**

| Iḍāfa | Transliteration | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| بَيْتُ مُحَمَّدٍ | baytu Muḥammadin | Muhammad's house |
| بَابُ الغُرْفَةِ | bābu al-ghurfati | the door of the room |
| كِتَابُ اللُّغَةِ العَرَبِيَّةِ | kitābu al-lugha al-ʿarabiyya | the Arabic language book |
| مَدِينَةُ القَاهِرَةِ | madīnatu al-Qāhirata | the city of Cairo |

**Rules:**
1. The **first noun loses its article and tanwīn** (no الـ, no ـٌ/ـٍ) — this signals it's in construct
2. The **second noun goes genitive** (ending in *-i*) and carries the definiteness for the whole phrase
3. Nothing can be inserted between the two nouns — no adjective, no article
4. Adjectives modifying the first noun must wait at the END of the iḍāfa chain

✗ *البَيْتُ الكَبِيرُ مُحَمَّدٍ* — can't insert adjective between
✓ *بَيْتُ مُحَمَّدٍ الكَبِيرُ* — Muhammad's big house (adjective follows the whole chain)

Iḍāfa is everywhere — it replaces prepositions, composes place names, creates compound concepts. Master it early.

# Questions <!-- slug: questions -->

## هَل and أَ — yes/no questions <!-- slug: question-hal -->

Two particles begin yes/no questions — they don't change word order:

**هَل (hal)** — neutral question marker (most common in MSA):
- هَل تَتَكَلَّمُ العَرَبِيَّةَ؟ (*Hal tatakallamu al-ʿarabiyya?*) — Do you speak Arabic?
- هَل أَنْتَ طَالِبٌ؟ (*Hal anta ṭālibun?*) — Are you a student?

**أَ (a-)** — prefixed directly to the first word (more formal/classical):
- أَتَفْهَمُ؟ (*atafahmu?*) — Do you understand?

Answer with the verb or a particle: **نَعَم** (*naʿam* — yes), **لَا** (*lā* — no), or echo the verb: *أَفْهَمُ* (I understand) / *لَا أَفْهَمُ*.

## Question words <!-- slug: question-words -->

Question words come at the beginning of the sentence:

| Arabic | Transliteration | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| مَن | man | who |
| مَا / مَاذَا | mā / mādhā | what |
| أَيْن | ayna | where |
| مَتَى | matā | when |
| كَيْف | kayfa | how |
| لِمَاذَا / لِمَ | limādhā / lima | why |
| كَم | kam | how much / how many |
| أَيّ | ayyu | which |

Examples:
- مَن هُوَ؟ (*Man huwa?*) — Who is he?
- مَاذَا تُرِيدُ؟ (*Mādhā turīdu?*) — What do you want?
- أَيْنَ تَسْكُنُ؟ (*Ayna taskunu?*) — Where do you live?
- كَيْفَ حَالُك؟ (*Kayfa ḥāluka?*) — How are you?
- كَم سَاعَةً دَرَسْتَ؟ (*Kam sāʿatan darastu?*) — How many hours did you study?

# Verbs — Past, Present & the Form System <!-- slug: verbs-past-present-forms -->

## The past tense (الماضي, al-māḍī) <!-- slug: verb-past -->

The past tense base = the root with the pattern **faʿala** (3rd person masculine singular) — this is also the dictionary citation form. Suffixes mark person, gender, and number:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 3rd m. | كَتَبَ (*kataba* — he wrote) | كَتَبُوا (*katabū*) |
| 3rd f. | كَتَبَتْ (*katabat*) | كَتَبْنَ (*katabna*) |
| 2nd m. | كَتَبْتَ (*katabta*) | كَتَبْتُمْ (*katabtum*) |
| 2nd f. | كَتَبْتِ (*katabti*) | كَتَبْتُنَّ (*katabtunna*) |
| 1st | كَتَبْتُ (*katabtu*) | كَتَبْنَا (*katabnā*) |

The past tense is fairly regular — once you know the root pattern, conjugation follows.

## The present tense (المضارع, al-muḍāriʿ) <!-- slug: verb-present -->

Present/imperfect is built with **prefixes + suffixes** on the root:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 3rd m. | يَكْتُبُ (*yaktubu*) | يَكْتُبُونَ (*yaktubūna*) |
| 3rd f. | تَكْتُبُ (*taktubu*) | يَكْتُبْنَ (*yaktubna*) |
| 2nd m. | تَكْتُبُ (*taktubu*) | تَكْتُبُونَ (*taktubūna*) |
| 2nd f. | تَكْتُبِينَ (*taktubīna*) | تَكْتُبْنَ (*taktubna*) |
| 1st | أَكْتُبُ (*aktubu*) | نَكْتُبُ (*naktubu*) |

The **three present vowel patterns** (faʿala → yafʿulu / yafʿalu / yafʿilu) must be learned per root — this is one reason dictionaries always give the present form alongside the past.

## Verb Forms I–X — the derivation system <!-- slug: verb-forms -->

Arabic derives new verbs from a root by applying one of **ten standard patterns** (sometimes called *mazīd* forms). Each form adds a predictable layer of meaning:

| Form | Pattern | Meaning shift | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | فَعَلَ faʿala | base action | كَتَبَ (wrote) |
| II | فَعَّلَ faʿʿala | causative / intensive | دَرَّسَ (taught — caused to learn) |
| III | فَاعَلَ fāʿala | mutual / with someone else | كَاتَبَ (corresponded with) |
| IV | أَفْعَلَ afʿala | causative | أَكْتَبَ (made write) |
| V | تَفَعَّلَ tafaʿʿala | reflexive of II | تَعَلَّمَ (learned — Form V of عَلَّمَ) |
| VI | تَفَاعَلَ tafāʿala | mutual/reciprocal | تَكَاتَبَ (wrote to each other) |
| VII | اِنْفَعَلَ infaʿala | passive / intransitive | اِنْكَسَرَ (got broken) |
| VIII | اِفْتَعَلَ iftaʿala | reflexive / self-directed | اِكْتَسَبَ (acquired for oneself) |
| X | اِسْتَفْعَلَ istafʿala | requestive / to consider | اِسْتَخْدَمَ (used/employed — from خَدَمَ, served) |

You don't need to produce all forms from scratch — but recognizing Form V (*ta-* prefix + doubled middle) or Form X (*ista-* prefix) lets you decode unfamiliar words immediately.

# Negation <!-- slug: negation -->

Arabic uses different negators for different tenses and moods:

| Negator | Used with | Example |
|---|---|---|
| لَا (lā) | present/future (indicative) / commands | لَا أَفْهَمُ — I don't understand |
| مَا (mā) | past tense (in some dialects/styles), nominal sentences | مَا فَهِمْتُ — I didn't understand |
| لَمْ (lam) + jussive | past negation (very common in MSA) | لَمْ أَفْهَمْ — I didn't understand |
| لَنْ (lan) + subjunctive | future negation | لَنْ أَذْهَبَ — I will not go |
| لَيْسَ (laysa) | negates nominal sentences (to be) | لَيْسَ هُوَ طَالِبًا — He is not a student |

**لَمْ + jussive** is the standard way to negate the past in MSA: the present-tense verb form is used but the final vowel drops or changes to sukūn:
- يَكْتُبُ (*yaktubu*, he writes) → لَمْ يَكْتُبْ (*lam yaktub*, he didn't write)

**لَيْسَ** conjugates like a past-tense verb:
- لَسْتُ (*lastu*) — I am not; لَسْتَ (*lasta*) — you are not; لَيْسَ (*laysa*) — he is not

# Prepositions (حروف الجر) <!-- slug: prepositions -->

Prepositions put the following noun into the **genitive case**. Common ones:

| Preposition | Transliteration | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| في | fī | in / at | في البَيْتِ — in the house |
| عَلَى | ʿalā | on | عَلَى الطَّاوِلَةِ — on the table |
| مِن | min | from / some of | مِن مِصْرَ — from Egypt |
| إِلَى | ilā | to / toward | إِلَى المَدْرَسَةِ — to school |
| عَن | ʿan | about / away from | سَأَلَ عَن — asked about |
| مَع | maʿa | with | مَعَهُ — with him |
| بِـ | bi- | with / by / in (attached) | بِالعَرَبِيَّةِ — in Arabic |
| لِـ | li- | for / to (attached) | لِي — for me |
| بَيْن | bayna | between | بَيْنَ البَيْتَيْنِ — between the two houses |
| أَمَام | amāma | in front of | أَمَامَ البَابِ — in front of the door |
| خَلْف | khalfa | behind | خَلْفَ الكُرْسِيِّ — behind the chair |
| بَعْد | baʿda | after | بَعْدَ الدَّرْسِ — after the lesson |
| قَبْل | qabla | before | قَبْلَ الامْتِحَانِ — before the exam |

**بِـ** and **لِـ** are inseparable prefixes — they attach directly to the next word: بِالكِتَابِ (with the book), لِلطَّالِبِ (for the student — note *li-l-* before the article).

# Comparatives — the أَفْعَل Pattern <!-- slug: comparatives -->

The comparative in Arabic follows a single, reliable pattern: **أَفْعَل (afʿal)** — the root with a prefix أَ and a fixed vowel structure:

| Adjective | Comparative | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| كَبِير (*kabīr*, big) | أَكْبَر (*akbar*) | bigger |
| صَغِير (*ṣaghīr*, small) | أَصْغَر (*aṣghar*) | smaller |
| كَثِير (*kathīr*, many) | أَكْثَر (*akthar*) | more |
| قَلِيل (*qalīl*, few) | أَقَل (*aqall*) | fewer / less |
| جَيِّد (*jayyid*, good) | أَفْضَل (*afḍal*) | better |
| سَرِيع (*sarīʿ*, fast) | أَسْرَع (*asraʿ*) | faster |
| جَمِيل (*jamīl*, beautiful) | أَجْمَل (*ajmal*) | more beautiful |

**Comparison structure: A أَكْبَر مِن B** = A is bigger than B
- مُحَمَّدٌ أَطْوَلُ مِن أَحْمَد — Muhammad is taller than Ahmad
- هَذَا الكِتَابُ أَفْضَلُ مِن ذَلِكَ — This book is better than that one

**Superlative**: use أَفْعَل + definite article OR أَفْعَل + noun in genitive:
- هُوَ الأَكْبَر — He is the biggest
- أَكْبَرُ مَدِينَةٍ — the biggest city

The أَفْعَل pattern is **invariable** in comparative use — it doesn't change for gender or number.

# Conditionals — إِذَا and لَوْ <!-- slug: conditionals -->

## إِذَا (idhā) — real / open conditionals <!-- slug: conditional-idha -->

Used when the condition is possible or likely (equivalent to "if" for plausible scenarios):

**إِذَا + past verb → result clause (past or present verb)**

- إِذَا دَرَسْتَ، نَجَحْتَ. (*Idhā darasta, najaḥta.*) — If you study, you will succeed.
- إِذَا كَانَ الجَوُّ جَمِيلًا، سَنَذْهَبُ. — If the weather is nice, we'll go.

The **past tense is used in the condition clause** even for future meaning — this is the standard Arabic conditional pattern.

## لَوْ (law) — unreal / counterfactual conditionals <!-- slug: conditional-law -->

Used for hypotheticals, wishes, and things contrary to fact:

**لَوْ + past verb → لَـ + past verb in result clause**

- لَوْ كَانَ عِنْدِي وَقْتٌ، لَسَافَرْتُ. (*Law kāna ʿindī waqtun, lasāfartu.*) — If I had time, I would travel.
- لَوْ كُنْتُ غَنِيًّا، لَاشْتَرَيْتُ بَيْتًا كَبِيرًا. — If I were rich, I'd buy a big house.

Note the **لَـ** prefix on the result clause of لَوْ conditionals — it's characteristic of this construction.

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

**Vocabulary:**
- فِيلْم (*fīlm*) — film; أَفْلَام (*aflām*) — films (broken plural!)
- مُخْرِج (*mukhrij*) — director (Form IV active participle of خَرَجَ)
- مُمَثِّل / مُمَثِّلَة (*mumaththil/mumaththila*) — actor/actress
- بَطَل / بَطَلَة (*baṭal/baṭala*) — hero/heroine, main character
- شَرِير (*sharīr*) — villain
- قِصَّة (*qiṣṣa*) — story; حَبْكَة (*ḥabka*) — plot
- نِهَايَة (*nihāya*) — ending; مَشْهَد (*mashhad*) — scene
- مُؤَثِّرَات بَصَرِيَّة (*muʾaththirāt baṣariyya*) — visual effects
- مُوسِيقَى (*mūsīqā*) — music/soundtrack

**Genre vocabulary:**
- فِيلْم رَوْمَانْسِيّ — romantic film
- فِيلْم إِثَارَة — thriller
- فِيلْم كَوْمِيدِيَا — comedy
- فِيلْم رُعْب — horror film
- فِيلْم خَيَال عِلْمِيّ — sci-fi film
- وَثَائِقِيّ (*wathāʾiqī*) — documentary

**Describing and opining:**

1. **Basics:** هَذَا الفِيلْمُ مِن إِخْرَاجِ... (*Hādhā al-filmu min ikhrāji...*) — This film is directed by...
2. **Plot:** يَتَحَدَّثُ الفِيلْمُ عَن... (*yataḥaddathu al-filmu ʿan...*) — The film is about... / تَدُورُ القِصَّةُ حَوْلَ... (*tadūru al-qiṣṣatu ḥawla...*) — The story revolves around...
3. **Opinion:** 
   - أَعْجَبَنِي هَذَا الفِيلْم. (*Aʿjabanī hādhā al-film.*) — I liked this film. (lit. "this film pleased me")
   - التَّمْثِيلُ رَائِع. (*at-tamthīlu rāʾiʿ.*) — The acting is wonderful.
   - الحَبْكَةُ مُثِيرَة للاهْتِمَام. (*al-ḥabkatu muthīratun lil-ihtimām.*) — The plot is interesting.
   - النِّهَايَةُ مُفَاجِئَة / مُتَوَقَّعَة. (*an-nihāyatu mufājiʾa / mutawaqaʿa.*) — The ending is surprising / predictable.
   - أَنْصَحُكَ بِمُشَاهَدَتِهِ. (*Anṣaḥuka bi-mushāhadatihi.*) — I recommend watching it.
   - يَسْتَحِقُّ المُشَاهَدَة. (*yastaḥiqqu al-mushāhada.*) — It's worth watching. (Form X verb!)
