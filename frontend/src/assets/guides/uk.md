# The Ukrainian Alphabet <!-- slug: alphabet -->

Ukrainian is written in **Cyrillic** — 33 letters. The alphabet itself takes a few days to learn; pronunciation is mostly regular after that.

## Letters unique to Ukrainian (vs Russian)

These four letters exist in Ukrainian but not Russian — they mark the language:

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **і** | і | "ee" as in *bee* | місто (misto) — city |
| **ї** | ї | "yee" — i with a built-in y glide | їхати (yikhaty) — to travel |
| **є** | є | "yeh" | єдиний (yedynyi) — only |
| **ґ** | ґе | hard "g" (never a fricative) | ґанок (hanok) — porch |

Russian uses и where Ukrainian uses и AND і depending on etymology. Ukrainian и is a shorter, more central sound than the Russian и.

## The apostrophe (апостроф)

Ukrainian uses an apostrophe ' (not a letter — a separator) between certain consonants and я/є/ї/ю to signal the consonant does NOT palatalize: *м'ясо (myaso — meat), п'ять (pyat — five), комп'ютер (kompyuter — computer)*. Think of it as a hard sign that's typographically light.

## Key sounds

- **и** — between the "i" in *bit* and the "u" in *but*. Shorter and more central than any English vowel.
- **х** — like the "ch" in Scottish *loch* or German *Bach*. Never "ks".
- **г** — a voiced version of х, a voiced velar fricative. Not the hard "g" — that's **ґ**.
- **ч** — "ch" as in *church*
- **ш** — "sh" as in *shop*
- **щ** — "shch", like *fresh cheese* said fast: *щастя (shchastya — happiness)*
- **ж** — "zh", like the "s" in *measure*
- **ц** — "ts" as in *cats*
- **дж** — "dj/j" as in *judge*
- Palatalization: many consonants before і, е, я, ю, є become "soft" (tongue touches palate). This is the main pronunciation challenge.
- Stress is not marked in ordinary text and must be learned per word — this is the #1 difficulty.

## Transliteration note

Throughout this guide: Ukrainian word (*romanization — English meaning*). Stress is marked with an accent when shown.

# No Articles, No Present "To Be" <!-- slug: no-articles-no-be -->

Two things Ukrainian learners notice immediately — and both are features, not bugs.

## No articles

Ukrainian has no *a, an, the* — none whatsoever. Context, word order, and demonstratives handle the job:

- *Я бачу кішку.* (Ya bachu kishku.) — I see a cat / the cat.
- *Ця кішка велика.* (Tsya kishka velyka.) — This cat is big.

When you translate from English, simply drop the article. When you translate into English, context decides which article to use.

## No present tense of "to be" (бути)

In the present tense, Ukrainian drops the copula entirely:

| Ukrainian | Transliteration | English |
|---|---|---|
| Я студент. | Ya student. | I am a student. |
| Він лікар. | Vin likar. | He is a doctor. |
| Вона гарна. | Vona harna. | She is beautiful. |
| Де книга? | De knyha? | Where is the book? |

- The copula **є** (ye) exists — it appears in definitions and philosophical statements: *Мова є душею нації.* (Language is the soul of a nation.) In everyday speech, є is often dropped.
- **бути** (buty) is fully conjugated in past and future: *був, була, було, були* (buv, bula, bulo, buly — was/were).
- In past and future you must use it: *Я був вдома.* (I was home.) *Вона буде тут.* (She will be here.)

✗ English trap: Don't try to insert an equivalent of "am/is/are" — the missing verb IS the correct form.

# Nouns, Gender & the Seven Cases <!-- slug: nouns-gender-cases -->

This is the structural heart of Ukrainian. Cases are not optional decoration — they replace prepositions and mark every grammatical relationship. Invest here and the rest of the language opens up.

## Gender

Three genders: **masculine, feminine, neuter**. Mostly predictable from the ending:

| Ending | Gender | Example |
|---|---|---|
| consonant | masculine | брат (brat — brother), стіл (stil — table) |
| -о, -е | neuter | вікно (vikno — window), поле (pole — field) |
| -а, -я | usually feminine | жінка (zhinka — woman), земля (zemlya — earth) |
| -ь (soft sign) | either (must learn) | ніч (nich — night, F); день (den — day, M) |

## The Seven Cases

Ukrainian has **7 cases**. Each encodes a grammatical role. You need to know all of them — they appear constantly.

**Core function summary:**

| # | Case | Ukrainian name | Key question | Core use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Nominative | Називний | Хто? Що? (Who? What?) | Subject of sentence |
| 2 | Genitive | Родовий | Кого? Чого? (Of whom? Of what?) | Possession, absence, quantity, after many prepositions |
| 3 | Dative | Давальний | Кому? Чому? (To whom? To what?) | Indirect object, beneficiary |
| 4 | Accusative | Знахідний | Кого? Що? (Whom? What?) | Direct object |
| 5 | Instrumental | Орудний | Ким? Чим? (With whom? With what?) | Means/instrument, accompaniment, after "to be" in predicate |
| 6 | Locative | Місцевий | На/В кому/чому? (In/on whom/what?) | Location (always with a preposition) |
| 7 | **Vocative** | **Кличний** | — (addressing someone) | **Direct address — unique and alive in Ukrainian** |

## The Vocative (кличний відмінок) — a Ukrainian pride

The **vocative** is the case for direct address. English has no equivalent — you just use the name. Ukrainian has dedicated endings, and using them is considered elegant and educated. Dropping the vocative in formal or literary contexts sounds foreign.

| Nominative | Vocative | Translation |
|---|---|---|
| мама (mama) | мамо (mamo) | Mom! |
| Андрій (Andrii) | Андрію (Andriyu) | Andrii! |
| Тарас (Taras) | Тарасе (Tarase) | Taras! |
| пан (pan — sir) | пане (pane) | Sir! |
| друг (druh — friend) | друже (druzhe) | Friend! |
| Україна (Ukraina) | Україно (Ukraino) | Ukraine! (in poetry) |

This is why Shevchenko's poems say *Україно* — it's the vocative, not a typo.

## Core noun endings — summary table (masculine, singular example: **брат** — brother)

| Case | Ending example | Form | Meaning note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | -Ø | брат | The brother (subject) |
| Genitive | -а/-я | брата | Of the brother / no brother |
| Dative | -у/-ю | брату | To the brother |
| Accusative | = nom (inanimate) | брата | See the brother |
| Instrumental | -ом/-ем | братом | With the brother |
| Locative | -у/-і (with prep) | (у) брату | About the brother |
| Vocative | -е/-у/-ю | брате | Brother! |

Feminine example: **сестра** (sestra — sister)

| Case | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Nominative | сестра | sister (subject) |
| Genitive | сестри | of the sister |
| Dative | сестрі | to the sister |
| Accusative | сестру | see the sister |
| Instrumental | сестрою | with the sister |
| Locative | (на) сестрі | about the sister |
| Vocative | сестро | Sister! |

## Plurals

Most plurals are formed by adding -и/-і (masculine/neuter), -и (feminine): *брати, вікна, сестри*. The case system applies equally to plurals — every case has a plural form too.

## Animacy

Animate masculine nouns (people, animals) have Accusative = Genitive: *Я бачу брата.* (Ya bachu brata — I see the brother.) Inanimate: Accusative = Nominative: *Я бачу стіл.* (I see the table.)

# Pronouns <!-- slug: pronouns -->

## Personal pronouns (declined)

Ukrainian personal pronouns decline through all seven cases. The nominative forms:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | я (ya — I) | ми (my — we) |
| 2nd | ти (ty — you) | ви (vy — you pl./formal) |
| 3rd m | він (vin — he) | вони (vony — they) |
| 3rd f | вона (vona — she) | — |
| 3rd n | воно (vono — it) | — |

**ви** (vy) is the formal singular "you" — use it with strangers, elders, officials. **ти** (ty) is informal. Mixing these up is a social faux pas in the wrong direction.

Key declined forms you'll encounter constantly:

| Nominative | Genitive | Dative | Accusative | Instrumental |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| я | мене | мені | мене | мною |
| ти | тебе | тобі | тебе | тобою |
| він | його | йому | його | ним |
| вона | її | їй | її | нею |
| ми | нас | нам | нас | нами |
| ви | вас | вам | вас | вами |
| вони | їх | їм | їх | ними |

After prepositions, 3rd-person pronouns add **н-**: *до нього (to him), у неї (at her place), з ними (with them)*.

## Possessive pronouns (agree in gender/number/case)

| English | M sg | F sg | N sg | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| my | мій | моя | моє | мої |
| your (sg) | твій | твоя | твоє | твої |
| his | його | його | його | його |
| her | її | її | її | її |
| our | наш | наша | наше | наші |
| your (pl/formal) | ваш | ваша | ваше | ваші |
| their | їхній | їхня | їхнє | їхні |

Possessives agree with the noun they modify — not the owner: *моя книга (my book, F), мій брат (my brother, M), моє вікно (my window, N)*.

# Question Formation <!-- slug: question-formation -->

## Three ways to ask a question

**1. Intonation** — the easiest. Take a statement, raise your voice on the key word:
*Ти знаєш Андрія?* (Ty znayesh Andriya?) — Do you know Andrii?

**2. The particle ЧИ (chy)** — placed at the start, signals yes/no question. More formal, unambiguous:
*Чи ти знаєш Андрія?* (Chy ty znayesh Andriya?) — Do you know Andrii?
*Чи вона прийде?* (Chy vona pryde?) — Will she come?

**3. Question words**

| Word | Translit | English |
|---|---|---|
| хто? | khto? | who? |
| що? | shcho? | what? |
| де? | de? | where? (location) |
| куди? | kudy? | where to? (direction) |
| звідки? | zvidky? | where from? |
| коли? | koly? | when? |
| чому? | chomu? | why? |
| як? | yak? | how? |
| який? яка? яке? | yakyy? | which? / what kind? |
| скільки? | skilky? | how many/much? |

Question words go to the front: *Куди ти йдеш?* (Where are you going?) *Що це таке?* (What is this?)

# Verb Aspect — Imperfective vs Perfective <!-- slug: verb-aspect -->

This chapter is the most important in the guide. Ukrainian does not have "tenses" the way English does — it has **aspect**. Until you internalize aspect, you're translating from English grammar that doesn't exist in Ukrainian.

## The core idea

Every Ukrainian verb exists in (at least) two forms:
- **Imperfective (недоконаний вид)** — the action as an **ongoing process**, **repeated habit**, or **general fact**. It doesn't tell you whether the action was completed.
- **Perfective (доконаний вид)** — the action as a **single completed event** with a **result**. It implies the action reached its goal.

| English | Imperfective | Perfective |
|---|---|---|
| to write | писати (pysaty) | написати (napysaty) |
| to read | читати (chytaty) | прочитати (prochytaty) |
| to speak | говорити (hovoryty) | сказати (skazaty) |
| to do/make | робити (robyty) | зробити (zrobyty) |
| to buy | купувати (kupuvaty) | купити (kupyty) |
| to come | приходити (prykhodуty) | прийти (pryyty) |

Typically a prefix transforms imperfective → perfective: *писати → на**писати***. Or a different root: *говорити (say things generally) → сказати (say one specific thing)*.

## What changes with aspect

**1. Present tense**: ONLY imperfectives have a present tense. Perfectives do not — if you conjugate a perfective in "present" endings, it means FUTURE.

**2. Past tense**: both aspects have past. The difference is narrative:
- *Вчора я **читав** книгу.* (Vchora ya chytav knyhu.) — Yesterday I was reading a book. (process, maybe didn't finish)
- *Вчора я **прочитав** книгу.* (Vchora ya prochytav knyhu.) — Yesterday I read (finished) the book.

**3. Future tense**: imperfective future uses *буду + infinitive*; perfective future conjugates like a present verb (see Future chapter).

## Choosing aspect in practice

| Situation | Aspect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Habit / repeated action | Imperfective | Я завжди **читаю** перед сном. (I always read before bed.) |
| Action in progress | Imperfective | Вона зараз **читає**. (She's reading now.) |
| General capability | Imperfective | Він добре **малює**. (He draws well.) |
| Single completed event | Perfective | Я **прочитав** цю книгу. (I've read / I read this book — done.) |
| Requesting a one-time action | Perfective | **Напиши** мені. (Write to me — once.) |
| Requesting an ongoing/repeated action | Imperfective | **Пиши** мені частіше. (Write to me more often.) |

✗ Trap: "I read Ukrainian every day" → imperfective: *Я читаю українською щодня.* NOT perfective.
✓ "I've read this article" → perfective: *Я прочитав цю статтю.*

Mnemonic: perfective = **P**oint in time, **P**roduct/result. Imperfective = **I**nfinite process, **I**teration.

# Present Tense <!-- slug: present-tense -->

Only imperfective verbs have a true present tense. Two conjugation classes:

## Class I — -е endings (most verbs)

**читати (chytaty — to read)**

| Person | Form | Translit |
|---|---|---|
| я (I) | читаю | chytayu |
| ти (you sg) | читаєш | chytayesh |
| він/вона/воно | читає | chytaye |
| ми (we) | читаємо | chytayemo |
| ви (you pl) | читаєте | chytayete |
| вони (they) | читають | chytayut |

## Class II — -и endings

**говорити (hovoryty — to speak)**

| Person | Form | Translit |
|---|---|---|
| я | говорю | hovoryu |
| ти | говориш | hovorysh |
| він/вона | говорить | hovoryt |
| ми | говоримо | hovorymo |
| ви | говорите | hovoryte |
| вони | говорять | hovoryat |

Common irregular presents:
- **бути (to be)**: є (ye) for all persons in affirmative (or omitted); **немає** (nemaye) = there is no
- **їсти (to eat)**: їм, їси, їсть, їмо, їсте, їдять
- **іти (to go on foot)**: іду, ідеш, іде, ідемо, ідете, ідуть
- **хотіти (to want)**: хочу, хочеш, хоче, хочемо, хочете, хочуть
- **дати (to give)**: дам, даси, дасть, дамо, дасте, дадуть

# Past Tense <!-- slug: past-tense -->

The Ukrainian past tense is built differently from any West European language — it **agrees with the subject in gender and number**, not in person. There is only one past form per gender/number.

| Subject gender/number | Ending | Example (читати) | Translit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular | -в | читав | chytav |
| Feminine singular | -ла | читала | chytala |
| Neuter singular | -ло | читало | chytalo |
| Plural (all genders) | -ли | читали | chytaly |

*Він читав книгу.* (He was reading / read a book.)
*Вона прочитала книгу.* (She read [finished] the book.)
*Ми говорили годину.* (We spoke for an hour.)

This means the verb gives you information about the subject's gender — useful when the subject is implied.

✗ Common mistake for English speakers: don't look for person agreement (no first/second/third distinction): *Я читав* (I read, masculine speaker) vs *Я читала* (I read, feminine speaker) — the verb changes with the speaker's gender.

For **бути**: *був (buv) / була (bula) / було (bulo) / були (buly)*.

# Future Tense <!-- slug: future-tense -->

Ukrainian has **two** future constructions, corresponding to the two aspects.

## Imperfective future — буду + infinitive

Describes an ongoing or repeated action in the future. Uses *буду/будеш/буде/будемо/будете/будуть* + imperfective infinitive:

*Я **буду читати** цей роман увесь тиждень.* — I will be reading this novel all week.
*Ми **будемо говорити** про це завтра.* — We will be talking about this tomorrow.

## Perfective future — synthetic (the distinctive form)

Describes a single completed future action. Formed by conjugating the perfective verb with the SAME endings as the present tense Class I or II — but it means future because perfectives have no present:

**написати (napysaty — to write [perfective])**

| Person | Form | Translit | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| я | напишу | napyshu | I will write |
| ти | напишеш | napyshesh | you will write |
| він/вона | напише | napyshe | he/she will write |
| ми | напишемо | napyshemo | we will write |
| ви | напишете | napyshete | you will write |
| вони | напишуть | napyshut | they will write |

*Я **напишу** тобі листа.* — I will write you a letter. (and I will finish it — result implied)
*Вона **прочитає** звіт до п'ятниці.* — She will read (finish reading) the report by Friday.

The synthetic perfective future is one of Ukrainian's most distinctive features — there's nothing quite like it in English.

# Verbs of Motion <!-- slug: verbs-of-motion -->

Ukrainian distinguishes how you move, not just where. This matters for prefix combinations.

## The key pairs

| Meaning | Unidirectional (one trip) | Multidirectional (repeated/general) |
|---|---|---|
| to go on foot | іти (ity) | ходити (khodyty) |
| to go by vehicle | їхати (yikhaty) | їздити (yizdyty) |
| to fly | летіти (lteity) | літати (litaty) |
| to swim/sail | плисти (plyty) | плавати (plavaty) |
| to carry (on foot) | нести (nesty) | носити (nosyty) |
| to carry (by vehicle) | везти (vezty) | возити (vozyty) |

**іти** — I am walking to the store right now (one trip, in progress).
**ходити** — I walk to work every day (general habit, repeated, or wander around).

*Зараз я **іду** до магазину.* (I am going to the store now.)
*Я **ходжу** до магазину щодня.* (I go to the store every day.)

## Prefixes transform motion verbs

Adding a prefix to the unidirectional form creates a perfective verb; adding it to the multidirectional creates an imperfective. Key prefixes:

| Prefix | Meaning | Imperfective (multidirectional) | Perfective (unidirectional) |
|---|---|---|---|
| при- | arrival | приходити (come [repeatedly]) | прийти (arrive) |
| ви- | exit | виходити (go out [habit]) | вийти (go out [once]) |
| за- | stopping by | заходити (stop by [habit]) | зайти (stop by [once]) |
| пере- | crossing | переходити (cross [habit]) | перейти (cross [once]) |
| від- | departure | відходити (leave [habit]) | відійти (leave [once]) |
| про- | passing by | проходити (pass by [habit]) | пройти (pass by [once]) |

*Він **зайшов** до кав'ярні.* (He stopped by the café — once, perfective.)
*Він **заходить** до нас щонеділі.* (He stops by every Sunday — habit, imperfective.)

# Adjectives & Adverbs <!-- slug: adjectives-adverbs -->

## Adjective agreement

Adjectives agree with the noun they modify in **gender, number, and case** — all three simultaneously. This is the main complexity of Ukrainian adjectives.

Long-form adjective endings (nominative):

| Gender/Number | Ending | Example (новий — new) |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular | -ий / -ій | новий будинок (new building) |
| Feminine singular | -а / -я | нова машина (new car) |
| Neuter singular | -е / -є | нове місто (new city) |
| Plural (all genders) | -і / -ї | нові книги (new books) |

Hard stems (-ий/-а/-е/-і), soft stems (-ій/-я/-є/-і).

*великий (velykyi — big): великий стіл, велика кімната, велике вікно, великі міста*

Adjectives follow the noun less often than in English — they usually precede.

## Comparison

- Comparative: adjective + **-іший / -ший**: *новіший (newer), більший (bigger from великий)*
- Or: **більш** + adjective: *більш складний (more complex)*
- Superlative: **най-** prefix: *найновіший (newest), найкращий (best)*
- Irregular: *гарний (beautiful) → кращий (better-looking) → найкращий; поганий → гірший → найгірший*

## Short-form (predicative) adjectives

In the predicate position after бути, some adjectives have short forms: *радий (glad), певний (sure), готовий (ready)*. These are used in fixed expressions.

## Adverbs

Most adverbs are formed from adjectives by replacing the ending with **-о**: *швидкий → швидко (quickly), добрий → добре (well/good), тихий → тихо (quietly)*.

Comparative adverb = comparative adjective (same form): *швидше (faster), краще (better), більше (more)*.

# Prepositions and the Cases They Govern <!-- slug: prepositions -->

Ukrainian prepositions are bound to specific cases — learning a preposition without its case is only half the information.

## Genitive prepositions

**до (to/until), від (from), без (without), для (for), після (after), під час (during), замість (instead of), крім (except), з/із (from, out of), біля (near), через (through/in [time])**

*до магазину* (to the store) | *від Андрія* (from Andrii) | *без молока* (without milk) | *для мами* (for mom) | *після роботи* (after work) | *біля університету* (near the university)

## Dative prepositions

**по (along/around, after — colloquial), завдяки (thanks to), всупереч (contrary to)**

*завдяки тобі* (thanks to you) | *по вулиці* (along the street)

## Accusative prepositions

**в/у (into, on — with motion), на (onto — with motion), за (behind/for — with motion), під (under — with motion), через (across, in [time]), про (about)**

*у ліс* (into the forest) | *на стіл* (onto the table) | *через тиждень* (in a week) | *про це* (about this)

## Instrumental prepositions

**з/із (with — together), між (between), над (above), під (under — static), перед (in front of), за (behind — static)**

*з другом* (with a friend) | *між будинками* (between the buildings) | *над містом* (above the city)

## Locative prepositions (always required with this case)

**в/у (in, at — static), на (on, at — static), при (at/by), по (after — formal)**

*у місті* (in the city) | *на столі* (on the table) | *при університеті* (at the university)

## Same preposition, different case, different meaning

**у/в**: *іти **в** ліс* [accusative — into the forest] vs *бути **в** лісі* [locative — in the forest]
**на**: *покласти **на** стіл* [accusative — onto the table] vs *лежати **на** столі* [locative — on the table]
**під**: *поставити **під** стіл* [accusative — under the table] vs *стояти **під** столом* [instrumental — standing under the table]

This motion/location split is consistent throughout Ukrainian.

# Numbers & Case Agreement <!-- slug: numbers-cases -->

Numbers in Ukrainian are grammatically demanding — they govern the noun's case differently depending on their value.

## Cardinal numbers

## The case rules (the hard part)

| Numeral | Case of following noun |
|---|---|
| 1 (один) | Nominative singular |
| 2, 3, 4 (два, три, чотири) | Genitive singular |
| 5–20, etc. | Genitive plural |
| 21, 22, 23, 24 | Follow the last digit (so 21 → nom sg, 22 → gen sg) |

*один студент* (one student, nom sg)
*два студенти* (two students, gen sg)
*п'ять студентів* (five students, gen pl)
*двадцять один студент* (twenty-one students, nom sg — follows "один")

When the whole noun phrase is NOT the subject, the number itself declines and takes the noun with it — this is advanced territory, but be aware cases apply to numerals too.

## Ordinal numbers

Form like adjectives and agree in gender/number/case:
*перший/перша/перше* (first), *другий* (second), *третій* (third), *четвертий* (fourth), *п'ятий* (fifth)

# Negation <!-- slug: negation -->

## Double negation — mandatory in Ukrainian

Ukrainian requires **double (or multiple) negation** — every negative element in the sentence takes negative form. This is not "bad grammar"; it is the rule.

*Я нічого не знаю.* (Ya nichoho ne znayu.) — I don't know anything. (literally: I nothing-GEN NOT know)
*Він нікуди не ходив.* (Vin nikudy ne khodyv.) — He didn't go anywhere. (literally: He nowhere NOT went)
*Ніхто нічого не сказав.* (Nikhto nichoho ne skazav.) — Nobody said anything.

Negative pronouns/adverbs:
- **ніхто** (nobody), **ніщо/нічого** (nothing), **ніде** (nowhere — location), **нікуди** (nowhere — direction), **ніколи** (never), **ніяк** (no way)

All of these MUST co-occur with **не** before the verb.

✗ English logic: "I don't know anything" → Ukrainian does NOT say *я не знаю щось* — that means something else.

## Genitive of negation (родовий заперечення)

When the verb is negated, the direct object often shifts from **Accusative** to **Genitive**:

*Я маю **книгу**.* (I have a book — accusative.) → *Я не маю **книги**.* (I don't have a book — genitive.)
*Він купив **хліб**.* (He bought bread — accusative.) → *Він не купив **хліба**.* (He didn't buy bread — genitive.)

This is not always obligatory (especially with specific/definite objects) but it's the dominant pattern.

## Немає — the key negative existential

**Немає** (nemaye) = there is no / there isn't:
*Немає часу.* (There is no time. — genitive: *часу*)
*Немає молока.* (There is no milk.)
*Мене немає вдома.* (I'm not home. — genitive of pronoun: *мене*)

The opposite is **є** (there is/are) or simply stated without є.

# Conjunctions & Complex Sentences <!-- slug: conjunctions -->

## Core conjunctions

| Ukrainian | Translit | English | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| і / й / та | i / y / ta | and | coordinating |
| але / проте | ale / prote | but | coordinating |
| або | abo | or | coordinating |
| бо / тому що | bo / tomu shcho | because | subordinating |
| хоча | khocha | although | subordinating |
| якщо | yakshcho | if | subordinating |
| коли | koly | when | subordinating |
| що | shcho | that | subordinating |
| щоб | shchob | so that / in order to | subordinating |
| який/яка/яке | yakyy | which/that (relative) | relative |

## що (that)

**що** introduces content clauses — it is NOT optional, unlike English "that":
*Я думаю, **що** він має рацію.* — I think (that) he is right.
*Вона сказала, **що** прийде.* — She said (that) she would come.

## щоб (so that / in order to)

**щоб** introduces purpose clauses and reported commands. It triggers the **past tense** in the subordinate clause (a quasi-subjunctive):
*Я прошу, **щоб** ти прийшов.* — I ask that you come / I want you to come.
*Він поїхав, **щоб** побачити її.* — He left in order to see her.

## який as a relative pronoun

**який/яка/яке/які** agrees in gender/number with its antecedent and declines for case in its own clause:
*Книга, **яку** я читаю, дуже цікава.* — The book (that) I'm reading is very interesting. (*яку* = accusative because it's the object of *читаю*)
*Хлопець, **якому** я допоміг, — мій сусід.* — The boy whom I helped is my neighbor. (*якому* = dative)

# Conditionals — би/б <!-- slug: conditionals -->

Ukrainian conditionals are formed with the **particle би (by) or б (b)** — the choice is purely phonetic (б after vowels, би after consonants). This particle combines with the past tense form.

## Real conditional (якщо… то)

For real/possible conditions, use **якщо (yakshcho — if)** + present/future:
*Якщо буде сонце, ми підемо в парк.* — If it's sunny, we'll go to the park.
*Якщо ти прийдеш, я буду рада.* — If you come, I'll be glad.

## Unreal/hypothetical conditional (би + past)

For unreal or hypothetical situations (both present-unreal and past-unreal — same form in Ukrainian):
**якби + past tense … би + past tense**

*Якби я знав, я б сказав тобі.* (Yakby ya znav, ya b skazav tobi.) — If I knew / had known, I would have told you.
*Якби вона прийшла, ми б поговорили.* — If she came / had come, we would have talked.

Notice: Ukrainian uses the **same construction** for what English splits into second and third conditionals. The past tense in the subordinate clause signals unreality, not past time.

**би/б** can appear in either or both clauses, and can be used without якби to express a polite wish:
*Я б хотів каву.* (Ya b khotiv kavu.) — I would like a coffee.
*Вона б поїхала в Київ.* — She would go to Kyiv.

# Imperatives <!-- slug: imperatives -->

## Formation

**Informal singular (ти)** — the base imperative:
- Class I verbs: remove -є from 3rd sg present, add -й: *читає → читай (read!)*
- Class II verbs: remove -ть from 3rd pl present, result is the stem: *говорять → говор- → говори (speak!)*; if stem ends in consonant cluster: *напишуть → напиш- → напиш!*

Common imperatives: *скажи (say!), іди (go!), дай (give!), будь (be!), дивись (look!), слухай (listen!), іди (go!), почекай (wait!)*

**Formal/plural (ви)** — add **-те** to the singular form:
*читайте, говоріть, скажіть, дайте, будьте*

## Let's — first person plural imperative

Add **-мо** or **-ймо** to the stem:
*Ходімо! (Khodimo!)* — Let's go!
*Говоримо. / Говорімо.* — Let's talk.
*Читаймо!* — Let's read!

## Softening imperatives

A bare imperative can sound rude. Add **будь ласка** (bud laska — please) or use the conditional to soften:
*Дай мені, будь ласка, …* — Please give me …
*Чи не міг би ти …?* — Could you possibly …?

# A Note on Ukrainian vs Russian <!-- slug: ukrainian-vs-russian -->

This note is for learners who approach Ukrainian knowing Russian — a common path.

**Do not assume transfer.** Ukrainian and Russian share Cyrillic and some vocabulary, but are distinct languages with different grammars, sounds, and lexicons.

Key structural differences:
- Ukrainian has the **vocative case**; Russian dropped it.
- Ukrainian has **і, ї, є, ґ**; Russian does not.
- Ukrainian **г** is a voiced fricative (like an English "h" but voiced); Russian г is a stop "g".
- Ukrainian keeps the **synthetic future** (*читатиму*); Russian uses only *буду читать*.
- Ukrainian has **more aspect pairs with lexical differentiation** rather than just prefixation.
- Many common words differ entirely: *говорити (Ukrainian) vs говорить (Russian — same); але (Ukrainian but) vs но (Russian but); та (Ukrainian and) — Russian rarely uses та*.
- False friends: Ukrainian **рот** (rot — mouth) vs Russian *год* (god — year); Ukrainian **луна** (luna — echo) vs Russian *луна* (luna — moon — same!); Ukrainian **неділя** (nedilya — Sunday) vs Russian *неделя* (nedelya — week).

If you're coming FROM Russian: the vocabulary overlap will help, but fight the urge to russify Ukrainian phonology (especially г vs ґ, and и vs і).

# Describing a Movie <!-- slug: describing-a-movie -->

Plot summary convention: use **present tense** (imperfective, ongoing) for narrating the story.

**Opening:** *Це фільм про…* (Tse film pro… — This is a film about…) | *Фільм розповідає про…* (The film tells about…) | *Дія відбувається в…* (The action takes place in…)

**Genre:** *комедія (comedy), трилер (thriller), драма (drama), бойовик (boyovik — action film), мелодрама (romance), фантастика (sci-fi), жах / фільм жахів (horror)*

**Narrating plot (present tense, imperfective):**
*Головний герой хоче…, але…* (The main character wants…, but…)
*У фільмі розповідається про жінку, яка…* (The film tells about a woman who…)
*Наприкінці…* (At the end…)

**Opinion vocabulary:**
- *Мені сподобався фільм.* (Meni spodobavsya film.) — I liked the film. (past perfective — single event of liking)
- *Фільм дуже цікавий / нудний / зворушливий / захоплюючий.* (interesting / boring / touching / gripping)
- *Гра акторів чудова.* (The acting is wonderful.)
- *Сюжет (syuzhet — plot), кінець (kinets — ending), саундтрек (soundtrack), спецефекти (special effects)*
- *Рекомендую подивитися.* (I recommend watching it.) — perfective, one-time action
- *Не варто дивитися.* (Not worth watching.)

**The -ий/-а trap in opinions:** Unlike English, the adjective ending changes with gender of the noun: *фільм цікавий* (M) but *книга цікава* (F) but *кіно цікаве* (N).
